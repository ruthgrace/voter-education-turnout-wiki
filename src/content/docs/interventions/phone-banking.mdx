**Phone contact** spans a wide quality range, and the turnout evidence tracks that range
almost perfectly. At the effective end, **volunteer phone banks** — real supporters having
unhurried, conversational calls — produce small-to-moderate turnout effects, meaningfully
larger than mail though smaller than door-knocking. At the ineffective end, **robocalls**
(automated pre-recorded messages) show turnout effects statistically indistinguishable from
zero across many experiments, making them one of the clearest null results in the GOTV
literature. Commercial, rushed, heavily scripted call-center calls fall in between, usually
closer to the null.

The explanation is the same mechanism that governs canvassing: what moves voters is the
degree of genuine, personal, two-way human interaction. A volunteer who sounds authentic
and engages in a brief real conversation activates the "personal ask" that drives turnout; a
robot reading a script activates almost nothing. This is why the single most reliable
predictor of a phone program's effect is not the message or the list but *how much the call
resembles a real conversation between two people*. Quality, pace, and authenticity dominate.

Phones, like canvassing and most mail, are a **mobilization** channel rather than an
education one. A good GOTV call nudges a leaning-or-decided citizen to actually show up; it
rarely changes what they believe or how informed they are. Phones can, however, be a useful
delivery mechanism for *procedural* information — polling location, hours, ID rules — which
lowers the cost of voting for people who intend to participate but face logistical friction.

## Background

| Call type | Personal quality | Turnout effect | Cost efficiency |
|---|---|---|---|
| Volunteer phone bank (quality) | High | Small–moderate | Moderate |
| Commercial call center (scripted) | Low | Small / near-null | Low–moderate |
| Robocall (automated) | None | ~Zero | Cheap but ineffective |

## Key Arguments

- **For (volunteer calls):** Scales more easily than door-knocking while retaining some personal-contact effect.
- **Against (robocalls):** Widely used yet essentially ineffective for turnout — a cautionary tale about intuition vs. evidence.
- **Design point:** Effect size rises with conversational quality; scripts that force speed and rigidity destroy the effect.

## Open Questions

- How much can training and screening raise the effect of paid call-center programs toward volunteer levels?
- Do calls that deliver procedural information outperform pure encouragement calls?
- Why do robocalls persist in campaigns despite consistent null findings — habit, cost, or measurement neglect?

## Key Research

Studies from the [Research Library](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/overview/) most relevant to this topic:

- [Gerber & Green (2000)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/gerber-green-2000-canvassing-phone-mail/) — finds impersonal commercial phone banks ineffective, setting up the later call-quality debate
- [Nickerson (2006)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/nickerson-2006-volunteer-phone-calls/) — evidence that personal, high-quality volunteer calls do increase turnout, revising the null phone-call finding
- [Nickerson (2007)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/nickerson-2007-quality-is-job-one-phone/) — establishes call quality/personalization as the decisive moderator of phone-bank effectiveness
- [Nickerson & Rogers (2010)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/nickerson-rogers-2010-voting-plan/) — shows implementation-intention ("voting plan") scripts outperform standard GOTV phone scripts
- [Green, McGrath & Aronow (2013)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/green-mcgrath-aronow-2013-field-experiments-review/) — summarizes modest average effects of calls, moderated by quality
- [Green & Gerber (Get Out the Vote)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/green-gerber-get-out-the-vote-book/) — consolidates evidence on volunteer vs. commercial calls and call quality
