**Voter guides and information tools** — nonpartisan pamphlets, official ballot guides,
online voting-advice applications (VAAs) like Germany's Wahl-O-Mat or the Dutch StemWijzer,
candidate comparison sites, and ballot-measure explainers — are the intervention family most
directly aimed at the wiki's second outcome: **how well votes match voter preferences**.
Unlike canvassing, mail, and texts, which mostly move *whether* people vote, information
tools try to improve *how* people vote by lowering the cost of learning what is on the
ballot and where candidates stand. This makes them the clearest bridge between "education"
and "voting" in the field-experiment tradition.

The evidence is more mixed and harder to interpret than for turnout tactics, for two
reasons. First, the outcome is subtler: measuring whether a vote better matches a voter's
"true" preferences is far harder than counting turnout. Second, information tools face a
selection problem — the people who seek out a voting-advice app are often already the most
engaged and informed, so real-world effects can look larger than the causal effect on a
random voter. Where researchers have used experiments or natural variation, VAAs and
information guides show measurable effects on *political knowledge*, on *vote choice*
(sometimes shifting support toward better-matching or smaller parties), and a modest,
inconsistent effect on *turnout* itself — engaged, informed citizens being somewhat more
likely to vote.

The upshot is a genuine trade-off in the intervention toolkit. Information tools are the
tactic most likely to improve the *quality* and preference-match of votes, but they are
among the *weakest* at raising raw turnout, and they tend to help the already-engaged more
than the disengaged. This is the practical expression of the
[information vs. mobilization](/key-debates/information-vs-mobilization/) debate: the tactics
that inform and the tactics that mobilize are largely different tools serving different
goals.

## Background

| Tool | Primary goal | Effect on knowledge | Effect on vote choice | Effect on turnout |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Official/nonpartisan ballot guide | Inform | Moderate | Modest | Small |
| Voting-advice application (VAA) | Match voters to parties | Moderate–high | Measurable (toward better match) | Small, mixed |
| Candidate comparison site | Inform | Moderate | Modest | Small |
| Ballot-measure explainer | Reduce confusion | Moderate | Fewer errors/roll-off | Minimal |

## Key Arguments

- **For:** The only tactic family that targets vote *quality* and preference-matching, not just participation.
- **Against:** Small and uncertain turnout effects; users self-select toward the already-informed, limiting reach.
- **Equity concern:** May widen gaps if only the engaged use them — unless paired with outreach to low-information voters.

## Open Questions

- Do information tools change outcomes among *disengaged* voters, or only reinforce the engaged?
- How do we credibly measure whether a vote better matches a voter's underlying preferences?
- Can information delivery be fused with mobilization tactics to get both reach and quality?

## Key Research

Studies from the [Research Library](/research/overview/) most relevant to this topic:

- [Converse (1990)](/research/converse-1990-popular-representation-information/) — frames the theoretical stakes of unequal information distribution
- [Page & Shapiro (1992)](/research/page-shapiro-1992-rational-public/) — frames how information moves collective (not just individual) preferences
- [Delli Carpini & Keeter (1996)](/research/delli-carpini-keeter-1996-what-americans-know/) — foundational evidence that political knowledge shapes whether citizens can identify and act on their interests
- [Bartels (1996)](/research/bartels-1996-uninformed-votes/) — quantifies the electoral stakes of voter information deficits
- [Lau & Redlawsk (1997)](/research/lau-redlawsk-1997-voting-correctly/) — establishes the outcome metric (correct voting) that information interventions aim to improve
- [Lau & Redlawsk (2001)](/research/lau-redlawsk-2001-cognitive-heuristics/) — implies simplified cues are not a costless replacement for substantive information
- [Meirick & Wackman (2004)](/research/meirick-wackman-2004-kids-voting-political-knowledge/) — curriculum functions as an information/engagement stimulus
- [Lassen (2005)](/research/lassen-2005-information-voter-turnout/) — supports information provision as a turnout lever
- [Larcinese (2007)](/research/larcinese-2007-political-knowledge-turnout/) — motivates information-based interventions to raise participation
- [Lau, Andersen & Redlawsk (2008)](/research/lau-andersen-redlawsk-2008-exploration-correct-voting/) — links the information environment to correct-voting rates
- [Garzia & Marschall (2012)](/research/garzia-marschall-2012-vaa-state-of-research/) — foundational review of VAAs as a scalable information/matching intervention
- [Gemenis & Rosema (2014)](/research/gemenis-rosema-2014-vaa-electoral-turnout/) — quasi-causal evidence that a voter-information tool increases turnout
