**Voter guides and information tools** — nonpartisan pamphlets, official ballot guides,
online voting-advice applications (VAAs) like Germany's Wahl-O-Mat or the Dutch StemWijzer,
candidate comparison sites, and ballot-measure explainers — are the intervention family most
directly aimed at the wiki's second outcome: **how well votes match voter preferences**.
Unlike canvassing, mail, and texts, which mostly move *whether* people vote, information
tools try to improve *how* people vote by lowering the cost of learning what is on the
ballot and where candidates stand. This makes them the clearest bridge between "education"
and "voting" in the field-experiment tradition.

The evidence is more mixed and harder to interpret than for turnout tactics, for two
reasons. First, the outcome is subtler: measuring whether a vote better matches a voter's
"true" preferences is far harder than counting turnout. Second, information tools face a
selection problem — the people who seek out a voting-advice app are often already the most
engaged and informed, so real-world effects can look larger than the causal effect on a
random voter. Where researchers have used experiments or natural variation, VAAs and
information guides show measurable effects on *political knowledge*, on *vote choice*
(sometimes shifting support toward better-matching or smaller parties), and a modest,
inconsistent effect on *turnout* itself — engaged, informed citizens being somewhat more
likely to vote.

The upshot is a genuine trade-off in the intervention toolkit. Information tools are the
tactic most likely to improve the *quality* and preference-match of votes, but they are
among the *weakest* at raising raw turnout, and they tend to help the already-engaged more
than the disengaged. This is the practical expression of the
[informed voting vs. maximum turnout](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/key-debates/informed-voting-vs-turnout/) debate: the tactics
that inform and the tactics that mobilize are largely different tools serving different
goals.

## Background

| Tool | Primary goal | Effect on knowledge | Effect on vote choice | Effect on turnout |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Official/nonpartisan ballot guide | Inform | Moderate | Modest | Small |
| Voting-advice application (VAA) | Match voters to parties | Moderate–high | Measurable (toward better match) | Small, mixed |
| Candidate comparison site | Inform | Moderate | Modest | Small |
| Ballot-measure explainer | Reduce confusion | Moderate | Fewer errors/roll-off | Minimal |

## Key Arguments

- **For:** The only tactic family that targets vote *quality* and preference-matching, not just participation.
- **Against:** Small and uncertain turnout effects; users self-select toward the already-informed, limiting reach.
- **Equity concern:** May widen gaps if only the engaged use them — unless paired with outreach to low-information voters.

## Ballot Completion and Roll-Off

A distinct and practical question — especially in long-ballot jurisdictions like San
Francisco, where voters face many local offices and numerous propositions — is whether voter
guides help people *finish* the ballot rather than skipping down-ballot races and measures.
This skipping is called **roll-off** (or undervoting), and it is heaviest exactly where the
information burden is highest: **ballot measures** show far more roll-off than top-of-ticket
contests
([Vanderleeuw & Engstrom 1987](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/vanderleeuw-engstrom-1987-race-referendums-rolloff/)).

Two mechanisms drive roll-off, and they have different implications for whether information
helps. The first is an **information/comprehension deficit**: voters "treat voting like a
test" and skip contests they feel unequipped to answer
([Wattenberg, McAllister & Salvanto 2000](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/wattenberg-mcallister-salvanto-2000-voting-sat-test/)),
and harder-to-read ballot questions produce measurably more roll-off
([Reilly & Richey 2011](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/reilly-richey-2011-ballot-question-readability-rolloff/); [Selb 2008](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/selb-2008-supersized-votes-ballot-length/)).
This part is what a plain-language voter guide is well suited to fix. The second is **choice
fatigue**: independent of knowledge, each additional decision earlier on the ballot raises
abstention later — [Augenblick & Nicholson (2016)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/augenblick-nicholson-2016-choice-fatigue/)
estimate that removing fatigue would cut abstentions by roughly 8% and would have flipped ~6%
of propositions. Information alone cannot fully overcome a fatigue effect caused by ballot
length itself.

The direct evidence that guides raise completion is **suggestive rather than settled**. The
strongest result is that **vote-by-mail increases ballot completion**, plausibly because
voting at home gives people the time and ability to look things up while marking the ballot
([Menger, Stein & Vonnahme 2018](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/menger-stein-vonnahme-2018-undervote-vote-by-mail/)),
and point-of-decision prompting (machines that flag skipped contests) also reduces roll-off
([Nichols & Strizek 1995](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/nichols-strizek-1995-voting-machines-rolloff/)).
But a key caution: information that voters must *actively seek out* does not help — newspaper
endorsements persuade but do **not** reduce roll-off, because chronic skippers are not the
people reading them
([Fahey, Weissert & Uttermark 2018](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/fahey-weissert-uttermark-2018-newspaper-endorsements-rolloff/)).
The implication for a place like San Francisco: a guide is most likely to raise completion
when it is *pushed to voters with the ballot* (e.g., mailed alongside it) and written in
plain language, not when it sits on a website the disengaged never visit. No randomized study
yet isolates the official voter pamphlet's effect on down-ballot completion — a real gap.

San Francisco's **ranked-choice ballots** add a related wrinkle. Their analog of roll-off is
the **exhausted ballot** — one whose ranked candidates are all eliminated before the final
round, so it stops counting. Across four Bay Area RCV contests these ran roughly 10–27% of
ballots, enough that the winner took office without a majority of ballots originally cast
([Burnett & Kogan 2015](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/burnett-kogan-2015-ballot-exhaustion/)),
and the extra complexity of ranking also raises marking errors that fall unevenly across voter
groups ([Neely & Cook 2008](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/neely-cook-2008-whose-votes-count/)).
See the [California & San Francisco](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/overview/) research
theme for the local RCV, vote-by-mail, and election-timing evidence.

## Open Questions

- Do information tools change outcomes among *disengaged* voters, or only reinforce the engaged?
- How do we credibly measure whether a vote better matches a voter's underlying preferences?
- Can information delivery be fused with mobilization tactics to get both reach and quality?
- Does the official voter guide, pushed to voters with the ballot, actually raise down-ballot and proposition completion — and how much of roll-off is information versus irreducible choice fatigue?

## Key Research

Studies from the [Research Library](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/overview/) most relevant to this topic:

- [Converse (1990)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/converse-1990-popular-representation-information/) — frames the theoretical stakes of unequal information distribution
- [Page & Shapiro (1992)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/page-shapiro-1992-rational-public/) — frames how information moves collective (not just individual) preferences
- [Lupia (1994)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/lupia-1994-shortcuts-encyclopedias/) — canonical evidence that endorsement/interest-group cues let low-information voters mimic informed ballot-measure choices
- [Delli Carpini & Keeter (1996)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/delli-carpini-keeter-1996-what-americans-know/) — foundational evidence that political knowledge shapes whether citizens can identify and act on their interests
- [Bartels (1996)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/bartels-1996-uninformed-votes/) — quantifies the electoral stakes of voter information deficits
- [Lau & Redlawsk (1997)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/lau-redlawsk-1997-voting-correctly/) — establishes the outcome metric (correct voting) that information interventions aim to improve
- [Lupia & McCubbins (1998)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/lupia-mccubbins-1998-democratic-dilemma/) — theoretical foundation for when cues and endorsements can substitute for detailed information
- [McDermott (1998)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/mcdermott-1998-race-gender-cues/) — documents demographic ballot cues as a low-cost information source, and implies substantive guides can counter stereotype-driven cue use
- [Kuklinski et al. (2000)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/kuklinski-2000-misinformation-currency/) — shows that giving correct facts alone frequently fails to correct entrenched misinformation
- [Wattenberg, McAllister & Salvanto (2000)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/wattenberg-mcallister-salvanto-2000-voting-sat-test/) — diagnoses information scarcity as the mechanism behind roll-off, motivating information-supply interventions
- [Lau & Redlawsk (2001)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/lau-redlawsk-2001-cognitive-heuristics/) — implies simplified cues are not a costless replacement for substantive information
- [Meirick & Wackman (2004)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/meirick-wackman-2004-kids-voting-political-knowledge/) — curriculum functions as an information/engagement stimulus
- [Matsusaka (2004)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/matsusaka-2004-for-the-many-or-the-few/) — evidence bearing on whether ballot-initiative electorates produce majority-congruent policy
- [Lassen (2005)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/lassen-2005-information-voter-turnout/) — supports information provision as a turnout lever
- [Larcinese (2007)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/larcinese-2007-political-knowledge-turnout/) — motivates information-based interventions to raise participation
- [Lau, Andersen & Redlawsk (2008)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/lau-andersen-redlawsk-2008-exploration-correct-voting/) — links the information environment to correct-voting rates
- [Boudreau (2009)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/boudreau-2009-closing-the-gap/) — shows a single trustworthy endorsement cue can raise low-sophistication voters' decision quality to the level of high-sophistication voters
- [Arceneaux & Kolodny (2009)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/arceneaux-kolodny-2009-educating-least-informed/) — real-world field evidence that group endorsements can align low-information voters with their own predispositions
- [Nyhan & Reifler (2010)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/nyhan-reifler-2010-when-corrections-fail/) — documents that factual corrections can fail or backfire, undermining information provision as a fix for misinformed voting
- [Bullock (2011)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/bullock-2011-elite-influence-informed-electorate/) — supports providing substantive policy information as a lever on opinion
- [Reilly & Richey (2011)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/reilly-richey-2011-ballot-question-readability-rolloff/) — implies that simplifying/explaining measure language (the job of a voter guide/plain-language summary) should raise proposition completion
- [Garzia & Marschall (2012)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/garzia-marschall-2012-vaa-state-of-research/) — foundational review of VAAs as a scalable information/matching intervention
- [Gemenis & Rosema (2014)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/gemenis-rosema-2014-vaa-electoral-turnout/) — quasi-causal evidence that a voter-information tool increases turnout
- [Boudreau & MacKenzie (2014)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/boudreau-mackenzie-2014-informing-the-electorate/) — shows substantive policy information independently shapes ballot-measure opinion even alongside party cues
- [Boudreau, Elmendorf & MacKenzie (2015)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/boudreau-elmendorf-mackenzie-2015-informing-electorates-election-law/) — field-realistic experiment showing nonpartisan voter guides and endorsements each improve preference-congruent vote choice
- [Menger, Stein & Vonnahme (2018)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/menger-stein-vonnahme-2018-undervote-vote-by-mail/) — strongest causal-leaning evidence that giving voters time and access to information while voting raises completion
- [Fahey, Weissert & Uttermark (2018)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/fahey-weissert-uttermark-2018-newspaper-endorsements-rolloff/) — boundary condition: information voters must actively seek out (newspaper endorsements) persuades but does not entice abstainers to complete measures
- [Boudreau, Elmendorf & MacKenzie (2019)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/boudreau-elmendorf-mackenzie-2019-roadmaps-to-representation/) — tests voter guides and interactive spatial-map tools, finding both raise preference congruence most for uninformed voters
- [Boudreau, Colner & MacKenzie (2021)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/boudreau-colner-mackenzie-2021-voter-guides-narrow-gap/) — experimental evidence that an issue voter guide disproportionately helps uninformed voters, narrowing the informed/uninformed gap
