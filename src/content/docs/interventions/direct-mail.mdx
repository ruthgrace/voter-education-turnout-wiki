**Direct mail** is the workhorse of political outreach: cheap to produce, easy to target,
and endlessly variable in message. In turnout terms, though, ordinary GOTV mail — a
postcard reminding someone that an election is coming and urging them to vote — has a
*small* effect, typically a fraction of a percentage point per piece. The value of standard
mail lies in scale and precision, not per-contact potency: it can reach hundreds of
thousands of targeted voters at a cost per vote that is real but unremarkable compared to
personal contact.

The important exception, and one of the most famous results in political science, is
**social-pressure mail**. Gerber, Green, and Larimer's 2008 "neighbors" experiment sent
mailers showing recipients their own past voting history (whether they voted, not how) and
that of their neighbors, with an implied promise to follow up after the election. This
produced an effect several times larger than any ordinary mailer — on the order of an
8-point turnout increase — by activating a powerful motivation: the desire not to be seen as
a non-participant. Softer variants ("you're being studied," "thank you for voting," self-
prediction prompts) capture part of this effect with less of the backlash that aggressive
shaming can provoke.

For this wiki, mail is almost purely a *mobilization* channel. Informational mailers can
carry content — where and how to vote, what is on the ballot — and overlap with voter guides
covered elsewhere, but the turnout-driving mechanism is reminder and pressure, not
education. Social-pressure mail in particular works by leveraging a social norm, which means
its effect on *who* turns out is broad and its effect on the *informational quality* of the
resulting vote is essentially nil.

## Background

| Mail type | Mechanism | Typical turnout effect | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Standard GOTV reminder | Salience / reminder | ~0.1–0.5 pts | Cheap, scalable, modest |
| Informational (how/where to vote) | Reduces procedural cost | Small | Overlaps with voter guides |
| "Thank you / you're studied" | Mild social norm | ~1–2 pts | Low backlash |
| Full social-pressure ("neighbors") | Strong social pressure | ~5–8 pts | Powerful but can anger recipients |

## Key Arguments

- **For:** Unmatched scale and targeting precision; social-pressure variants rival personal contact per dollar.
- **Against:** Ordinary mail is weak; strong social-pressure mail risks backlash and complaints.
- **Ethical note:** Displaying voting history is legal (participation is public) but feels invasive to many recipients.

## Open Questions

- How far can social-pressure effects be pushed before backlash cancels the gains?
- Do social-pressure effects persist across elections or fade once the novelty wears off?
- Can informational mail meaningfully improve vote *quality*, not just reduce procedural friction?

## Key Research

Studies from the [Research Library](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/overview/) most relevant to this topic:

- [Gerber & Green (2000)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/gerber-green-2000-canvassing-phone-mail/) — shows direct mail has small but measurable per-piece turnout effects
- [Gerber, Green & Shachar (2003)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/gerber-green-shachar-2003-voting-habit-forming/) — mail as an experimental treatment arm
- [Gerber, Green & Larimer (2008)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/gerber-green-larimer-2008-social-pressure/) — shows mail can produce very large effects when it applies social pressure
- [Panagopoulos (2010)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/panagopoulos-2010-pride-shame/) — additional evidence on how message framing conditions mail effectiveness
- [Green, McGrath & Aronow (2013)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/green-mcgrath-aronow-2013-field-experiments-review/) — aggregates small average mail effects across many experiments
- [Green & Gerber (Get Out the Vote)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/green-gerber-get-out-the-vote-book/) — reviews when and how mail (including social-pressure mail) works
