**Digital and social-media outreach** — display and video ads, targeted social ads, and
platform-native civic nudges — is the fastest-growing and most heavily funded channel in
modern campaigns, and also one of the *least well-supported* by rigorous turnout evidence.
The gap between spending and demonstrated effect is the defining feature of this area.
Well-identified field experiments on online *advertising* frequently find turnout (and
persuasion) effects that are small, statistically fragile, or indistinguishable from zero,
even as campaigns pour resources into the channel on the assumption that reach equals impact.

The most-cited exception cuts the other way and is instructive. Facebook's 2010 "61-million-
person experiment" showed that a **social** message — an "I Voted" button displaying which of
a user's *friends* had voted — produced a small but real increase in turnout, while an
informational-only message did not. The lesson mirrors the rest of the GOTV literature:
what worked was the **social** signal (seeing that real friends voted), not the ad content.
Pure informational or persuasive digital ads, by contrast, have a weak track record, and
concerns about measurement — self-reported outcomes, targeting confounds, and platform-
controlled data — make many optimistic industry claims hard to verify independently.

For this wiki, digital ads are mostly a **mobilization/persuasion** channel with an
unusually poor evidence-to-usage ratio. Their relevance to vote *quality* is double-edged:
the same targeting infrastructure that can deliver useful procedural or comparative
information can also deliver micro-targeted, low-accountability persuasion and
misinformation. This makes digital outreach the intervention where the tension between
informing voters and merely nudging them is most fraught and least settled.

## Background

| Digital tactic | Turnout evidence | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Social "friends voted" nudge | Small positive (real) | Effect from social signal, not ad content |
| Targeted display/video ads | Small / near-null | Heavily used, weakly proven |
| Persuasion ads | Near-null on average | Effects tiny and hard to detect |
| Procedural/info ads | Plausible small benefit | Under-studied; overlaps with voter guides |

## Key Arguments

- **For:** Enormous, cheap reach and precise targeting; the social-nudge variant genuinely works.
- **Against:** Most measured effects are small or null; industry claims often outrun credible evidence.
- **Caution:** Micro-targeting enables low-accountability persuasion and misinformation, raising quality and integrity concerns.

## Open Questions

- How large is the *causal* turnout effect of ordinary digital ads once selection and measurement bias are removed?
- Can the social-signal mechanism be harnessed responsibly without manipulating users' feeds?
- Does digital micro-targeting, on net, inform voters or degrade the informational quality of votes?

## Key Research

Studies from the [Research Library](/research/overview/) most relevant to this topic:

- [Bond et al. (2012)](/research/bond-2012-61-million-person-experiment/) — flagship large-scale experiment showing social-media civic nudges can mobilize turnout
- [Garzia & Marschall (2012)](/research/garzia-marschall-2012-vaa-state-of-research/) — situates VAAs within online political information tools
- [Broockman & Green (2014)](/research/broockman-green-2014-online-advertisements-name-recognition/) — early rigorous null on the persuasive/recognition effects of online display ads
- [Gemenis & Rosema (2014)](/research/gemenis-rosema-2014-vaa-electoral-turnout/) — evaluates a digital tool's effect on participation
- [Hersh (2015)](/research/hersh-2015-hacking-the-electorate/) — explains the data infrastructure and limits behind microtargeted digital outreach
- [Jones et al. (2017)](/research/jones-2017-social-influence-2012-election/) — replicates social-media mobilization effects in a presidential-year context
- [Kalla & Broockman (2018)](/research/kalla-broockman-2018-minimal-persuasive-effects/) — includes advertising among channels found to have near-zero general-election persuasion
- [Coppock, Hill & Vavreck (2020)](/research/coppock-hill-vavreck-2020-small-effects-advertising/) — large evidence base that advertising persuasion is uniformly small
- [Coppock, Green & Porter (2022)](/research/coppock-green-porter-2022-digital-advertising-vote-choice/) — field (behavioral, not survey) null on digital ad persuasion of actual vote share
- [Aggarwal et al. (2023)](/research/aggarwal-2023-campaign-wide-digital-advertising-turnout/) — largest campaign-wide field test showing near-zero average turnout effect of digital ads
