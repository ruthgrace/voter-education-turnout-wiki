Interventions are the targeted, campaign-style tactics used to mobilize or inform specific
voters — as opposed to the system-wide rules covered under Policy Approaches. This is the
best-evidenced area in the entire field, because most interventions can be, and have been,
tested with **randomized controlled trials (RCTs)**. Since Gerber and Green's foundational
field experiments, thousands of trials have measured what actually moves turnout, producing
unusually credible estimates of effect sizes and cost-effectiveness. The headline lesson is
sobering: most tactics produce *small* effects, personal contact beats impersonal contact,
and many popular tactics (robocalls, most digital ads) barely move turnout at all.

A crucial distinction runs through this section. Most of these tactics are **mobilization**
tools — they change *whether* someone votes — not **education** tools that change *how* they
vote or how informed they are. Door-knocking, mail, and texts mostly nudge already-decided
citizens across the participation threshold. Only a subset — voter guides, informational
mail, and school-based civics — attempt to improve the *content* of the vote. This is why
an intervention can be highly effective at raising turnout while being nearly irrelevant to
the question of whether votes match preferences, a tension explored in the
[informed voting vs. maximum turnout](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/key-debates/informed-voting-vs-turnout/) debate.

The table below ranks the major intervention families by the two metrics that matter most
in the applied literature: the typical effect on turnout and the approximate cost per
additional vote produced. Each linked page examines the mechanism, the strength of the
evidence, and the tactic's relevance to vote quality in more detail.

## Comparing Interventions

| Intervention | Personal? | **Typical turnout effect** | Approx. cost per vote | Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Door-to-door canvassing | High | **Large (~1 vote / ~14 contacts)** | Moderate–high | Very strong |
| Social-pressure mail | Impersonal | **Large for mail** | Low–moderate | Very strong |
| Phone banking (volunteer) | Medium | **Small–moderate** | Moderate | Strong |
| Text messaging (SMS) | Low | **Small but cheap** | Very low | Strong |
| Standard direct mail | Impersonal | **Small** | Moderate | Strong |
| Voter guides / information | Varies | **Small; affects choice** | Low | Moderate |
| Digital / social ads | Impersonal | **Near zero–tiny** | Varies | Growing |
| Robocalls | Impersonal | **~Zero** | Low | Strong (zero effect) |
| School-based civic education | High (long-run) | **Small, delayed** | High | Weak–moderate |

## How to Read the Evidence

- **Personal contact wins.** The more a tactic resembles a real conversation, the larger its effect.
- **Cheap ≠ ineffective per dollar.** Texts move few voters each but cost so little they can be efficient at scale.
- **Beware the null results.** Robocalls and most persuasion ads show near-zero turnout effects despite heavy use.

## Open Questions

- Which tactics move *vote choice* and information, not just participation?
- Do effects persist to the next election (habit formation) or decay immediately?
- How much do effect sizes shrink as tactics scale from experiments to full campaigns?

## Key Research

Studies from the [Research Library](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/overview/) most relevant to this topic:

- [Gerber & Green (2000)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/gerber-green-2000-canvassing-phone-mail/) — foundational modern GOTV field experiment establishing the randomized-experiment methodology for comparing mobilization tactics
- [Galston (2001)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/galston-2001-political-knowledge-engagement-civic-education/) — frames why political knowledge is a target for interventions
- [Green, Gerber & Nickerson (2003)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/green-gerber-nickerson-2003-local-elections-canvassing/) — demonstrates external validity and replicability of GOTV field-experiment findings
- [Gerber, Green & Shachar (2003)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/gerber-green-shachar-2003-voting-habit-forming/) — establishes downstream/persistent returns to any turnout intervention
- [Kam & Palmer (2008)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/kam-palmer-2008-reconsidering-education-participation/) — frames the causal-identification debate over education and turnout
- [Lau, Andersen & Redlawsk (2008)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/lau-andersen-redlawsk-2008-exploration-correct-voting/) — identifies contextual factors that improve vote quality, relevant to intervention design
- [Nickerson & Rogers (2010)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/nickerson-rogers-2010-voting-plan/) — introduces psychological plan-making as a scalable mechanism to boost mobilization effects
- [Sondheimer & Green (2010)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/sondheimer-green-2010-education-turnout-experiments/) — anchors the education-as-turnout-driver claim in experimental design
- [Green, McGrath & Aronow (2013)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/green-mcgrath-aronow-2013-field-experiments-review/) — meta-analytic benchmark of average effect sizes across all major personal-contact GOTV modes
- [Hersh (2015)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/hersh-2015-hacking-the-electorate/) — foundational account of how campaigns select and reach voters for contact
- [Broockman & Kalla (2016)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/broockman-kalla-2016-durably-reducing-transphobia/) — landmark for persuasion-via-conversation as a distinct mechanism from turnout mobilization
- [Flynn, Nyhan & Reifler (2017)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/flynn-nyhan-reifler-2017-nature-origins-misperceptions/) — synthesizes why information-based corrective interventions are constrained
- [Kalla & Broockman (2018)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/kalla-broockman-2018-minimal-persuasive-effects/) — synthesizes the persuasive impact of contact and advertising across many interventions
- [Green & Gerber (Get Out the Vote)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/green-gerber-get-out-the-vote-book/) — comprehensive synthesis and cost-per-vote comparison of all major mobilization tactics
- [Kalla & Broockman (2020)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/kalla-broockman-2020-reducing-exclusionary-attitudes/) — reinforces interpersonal conversation as a durable persuasion tool
- [Aggarwal et al. (2023)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/aggarwal-2023-campaign-wide-digital-advertising-turnout/) — benchmark for the limited real-world impact of paid digital outreach
