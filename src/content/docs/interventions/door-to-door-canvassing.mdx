**Door-to-door canvassing** — volunteers or paid canvassers knocking on doors to encourage
voting — is the most reliably effective get-out-the-vote tactic in the experimental
literature, and it set the template for the entire field-experiment tradition. Gerber and
Green's 1998 New Haven study, the origin point of modern GOTV research, found that
face-to-face contact raised turnout substantially where phone calls and mail did much less.
The durable rule of thumb from the accumulated trials is roughly **one additional vote for
every ~14 people successfully contacted** at the door, though estimates vary with context,
canvasser quality, and baseline turnout.

The reason canvassing works is mechanism, not message. What moves voters is the *personal,
interactive, hard-to-ignore* nature of a real human conversation — social connection and
the sense of being personally asked. Consistent with this, high-quality, unhurried
conversations outperform scripted door-drops, and canvassing is most effective among
lower-propensity voters who are on the margin of participating. Effects are strongest when
canvassing happens close to Election Day. Notably, the *content* of the script matters far
less than the *contact* itself for turnout — a key clue that canvassing is a mobilization
tool, not primarily an education tool.

That distinction is central to this wiki. Standard GOTV canvassing changes *whether* people
vote, not *how* they vote or how informed they are. A separate and more demanding strand of
research — deep canvassing — uses long, empathetic conversations to shift *attitudes* and
has shown promise on persuasion, but it is costlier and aimed at opinion change rather than
turnout. For the question of matching votes to preferences, ordinary canvassing is close to
neutral: it brings marginal voters to the polls without necessarily improving the
informational basis of their choice.

## Background

| Contact type | Relative effect | Cost | Best use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Face-to-face, quality conversation | Largest | Highest | Marginal, low-propensity voters |
| Scripted door-drop / lit only | Small | Low | Broad, low-intensity coverage |
| Deep canvassing (persuasion) | Attitude change | Very high | Opinion shift, not turnout |

## Key Arguments

- **For:** Largest and most robust turnout effect per contact; works especially on the voters other tactics miss.
- **Against:** Labor-intensive and expensive to scale; contact rates are low, so cost-per-vote can be high.
- **Nuance:** Canvasser quality and authenticity matter more than the script — this is a relationship effect, not an information effect.

## Open Questions

- How much of canvassing's effect is durable habit formation versus a one-time nudge?
- Can the "personal ask" mechanism be replicated cheaply at scale without losing potency?
- Does mobilizing marginal voters change outcomes, or just turnout levels?

## Key Research

Studies from the [Research Library](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/overview/) most relevant to this topic:

- [Gerber & Green (2000)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/gerber-green-2000-canvassing-phone-mail/) — core evidence that personal canvassing is the most effective mobilization mode (~8-point boost)
- [Green, Gerber & Nickerson (2003)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/green-gerber-nickerson-2003-local-elections-canvassing/) — multi-site replication confirming canvassing effectiveness across varied local elections
- [Gerber, Green & Shachar (2003)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/gerber-green-shachar-2003-voting-habit-forming/) — canvassing as the mobilizing treatment with downstream persistence
- [Nickerson (2008)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/nickerson-2008-is-voting-contagious/) — shows canvassing effects spill over within households, amplifying its per-contact impact
- [Green, McGrath & Aronow (2013)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/green-mcgrath-aronow-2013-field-experiments-review/) — quantifies canvassing as the highest-impact mode across studies
- [Nickerson (2015)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/nickerson-2015-voter-registration-drives/) — experimental evidence that registration canvassing adds new voters
- [Broockman & Kalla (2016)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/broockman-kalla-2016-durably-reducing-transphobia/) — foundational deep-canvassing experiment showing durable attitude change via face-to-face conversation
- [Kalla & Broockman (2018)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/kalla-broockman-2018-minimal-persuasive-effects/) — canvassing among the contact modes estimated to have minimal persuasive (as opposed to mobilizing) effect
- [Green & Gerber (Get Out the Vote)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/green-gerber-get-out-the-vote-book/) — summarizes evidence and best practices for canvassing
- [Kalla & Broockman (2020)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/kalla-broockman-2020-reducing-exclusionary-attitudes/) — establishes narrative exchange (not argumentation) as the active ingredient in deep canvassing, and shows replicability
