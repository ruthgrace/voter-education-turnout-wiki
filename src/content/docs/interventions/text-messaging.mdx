**Text-message (SMS) reminders** occupy a distinctive spot in the GOTV toolkit: a *small*
effect per recipient but a *tiny* cost, which can make them among the most cost-effective
tactics per additional vote when deployed at scale. The pioneering experiment by Dale and
Strauss found that a simple, non-partisan reminder text raised turnout by roughly a
percentage point — modest in absolute terms but large relative to the near-zero cost of
sending a message. Subsequent trials have largely confirmed a small positive effect, with
important conditions.

The key finding, framed by Dale and Strauss as the **"noticeable reminder"** theory, is
that texts work primarily by cutting through inattention: many people intend to vote but
forget, and a well-timed nudge closes that intention-action gap. This implies that texts
help most among voters who are already registered and inclined to vote, and least among the
genuinely undecided or unmotivated. Effectiveness is sensitive to consent and relationship —
texts from a known, opted-in sender (a campaign or organization the recipient recognizes)
outperform cold, unsolicited blasts, which can annoy recipients and, with modern spam
filtering and regulation, may not even arrive.

For the wiki's central question, SMS is a pure **mobilization** and **reminder** channel: it
changes *whether* someone remembers to vote, not *how informed* their vote is. Its natural
extension into vote quality is procedural — texts can deliver polling-place lookups, hours,
and deadlines — which reduces the logistical cost of voting for the already-decided rather
than improving the informational content of the choice.

## Background

| Factor | Effect on SMS impact |
|---|---|
| Prior consent / known sender | Substantially higher effect and deliverability |
| Timing (close to Election Day) | Higher — leverages the reminder mechanism |
| Personalization | Modest positive |
| Cold, unsolicited blast | Low; risk of annoyance and spam filtering |
| Cost per message | Very low — drives strong cost-per-vote efficiency |

## Key Arguments

- **For:** Extremely cheap and scalable; strong cost-per-vote even with a small per-message effect.
- **Against:** Effect is small and fragile, concentrated among already-likely voters; spam rules and fatigue erode it.
- **Mechanism clarity:** Works as a reminder, not a persuader — do not expect it to move the undecided.

## Open Questions

- How quickly does message fatigue set in as texting saturates the electorate?
- Do procedural (where/how to vote) texts outperform pure encouragement texts?
- Can conversational/relational texting (two-way, peer-to-peer) recapture some personal-contact effect?

## Key Research

Studies from the [Research Library](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/overview/) most relevant to this topic:

- [Dale & Strauss (2009)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/dale-strauss-2009-text-message-reminders/) — foundational experiment establishing SMS reminders as a cost-effective mobilization tool
- [Malhotra et al. (2011)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/malhotra-michelson-rogers-valenzuela-2011-text-messages/) — demonstrates standalone "cold" SMS still mobilizes, strengthening the case for low-cost texting
- [Green, McGrath & Aronow (2013)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/green-mcgrath-aronow-2013-field-experiments-review/) — pools SMS experiments into an average mobilization estimate
- [Green & Gerber (Get Out the Vote)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/green-gerber-get-out-the-vote-book/) — summarizes SMS mobilization evidence and cost-effectiveness
