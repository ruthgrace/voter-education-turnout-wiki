Most of this wiki treats the ballot as fixed and asks how to get more (or better-informed)
voters to it. **Electoral-system** reforms change the ballot itself — the rule for translating
votes into winners. The main alternatives to simple plurality ("first past the post") are
**ranked-choice / instant-runoff voting (RCV)**, **approval voting**, and **proportional
representation (PR)**. Each is promoted as improving turnout, representation, or the match
between votes and preferences, and each is directly relevant here because the counting rule
determines both *whether a vote counts* and *how fully it expresses a preference*. The San
Francisco Bay Area is one of the most-studied natural laboratories, so much of the evidence is
local — see the [California & San Francisco](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/overview/)
research theme.

On **turnout**, the evidence is real but conditional. PR is fairly robustly associated with
higher turnout than plurality across democracies
([Blais & Carty](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/blais-carty-1990-pr-turnout/); [Lijphart](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/lijphart-patterns-of-democracy/)),
but the cleanest quasi-experiment finds PR *redistributes* mobilization across districts rather
than lifting it everywhere ([Cox, Fiva & Smith](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/cox-fiva-smith-2016-contraction-effect/)).
For RCV the picture is mixed: national panel work links RCV to higher municipal and youth
turnout ([Dowling et al. 2024](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/dowling-2024-rcv-turnout-mobilization/)),
yet a San Francisco study found mayoral turnout *declined* after RCV adoption, unevenly across
racial groups ([McDaniel](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/mcdaniel-2016-writing-the-rules-racial-turnout/)).

On **matching votes to preferences**, alternative methods look better in theory but carry a
usability cost. Approval and evaluative voting let voters express fuller preferences and
systematically shift winners from polarizing toward broadly-acceptable candidates
([Laslier & Van der Straeten](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/laslier-vanderstraeten-approval-2008/); [Baujard et al.](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/baujard-evaluative-voting-2014/)).
PR improves how well *legislatures* mirror the electorate, but that advantage shrinks toward
zero once actual coalition *governments* are the benchmark — a live scholarly dispute between
[Powell](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/powell-2000-elections-instruments/) /
[Golder & Stramski](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/golder-stramski-2010-congruence/) and
[Blais & Bodet](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/blais-bodet-2006-congruence/). And the
usability cost is not trivial: ranked ballots are roughly ten times more likely to be rejected
for marking errors, with errors concentrated among lower-income, less-educated, and minority
voters ([Pettigrew & Radley](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/pettigrew-radley-2025-mismarked-rcv-ballots/)),
and RCV's *exhausted ballots* mean winners can lack a true majority of ballots cast
([Burnett & Kogan](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/burnett-kogan-2015-ballot-exhaustion/)).

## Comparing Systems

| System | Turnout vs. plurality | Preference expression | Main cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| Plurality (first past the post) | Baseline | One choice; wasted votes | Spoiler effects, tactical voting |
| Ranked-choice / IRV | Mixed (context-dependent) | Rank several; fuller | Marking errors, exhausted ballots |
| Approval voting | Not established | Approve any number; simple | Less real-world evidence |
| Proportional representation | Higher (but redistributes) | Seats ≈ vote shares | Congruence gain fades at govt level |

## Key Arguments

- **For alternatives:** Reduce wasted votes and spoiler effects; approval/PR shift outcomes toward broadly-preferred, more representative winners; PR raises turnout on average.
- **Against / cautions:** Ranked and complex ballots raise error and rejection rates that fall hardest on marginalized voters; RCV turnout effects are inconsistent; PR's representation edge is contested at the governing stage.
- **Usability is the crux:** The preference-expression gains only materialize if voters can mark the ballot correctly — making plain-language guidance and ballot design (see [Voter Guides and Information](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/interventions/voter-guides-and-information/)) part of any reform.

## Open Questions

- Does RCV raise turnout, lower it, or neither — and why do local studies disagree?
- Can approval voting's theoretical advantages be confirmed in real US elections (Fargo, St. Louis)?
- Who bears the usability cost of complex ballots, and can design/education close that gap?

## Key Research

Studies from the [Research Library](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/overview/) most relevant to this topic:

- [Blais & Carty (1990)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/blais-carty-1990-pr-turnout/) — classic cross-national evidence that PR electoral rules raise turnout relative to plurality
- [Powell (2000)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/powell-2000-elections-instruments/) — foundational framework contrasting majoritarian vs. proportional systems on representation
- [Blais & Bodet (2006)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/blais-bodet-2006-congruence/) — evidence that PR does not robustly improve citizen–government congruence
- [Farrell & McAllister (2006)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/farrell-mcallister-2006-preferential-satisfaction/) — evidence that ballot structure (preferential voting/STV), not just PR, shapes how voters experience elections
- [Brams & Fishburn (1983/2007)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/brams-fishburn-approval-voting/) — defines approval voting as an alternative to plurality and lays out its claimed representational advantages
- [Neely & Cook (2008)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/neely-cook-2008-whose-votes-count/) — SF evidence that ranked-choice ballots raise overvote rates and change error patterns across voter groups
- [Laslier & Van der Straeten (2008)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/laslier-vanderstraeten-approval-2008/) — real-electorate evidence that approval voting is implementable and changes who wins relative to plurality
- [Golder & Stramski (2010)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/golder-stramski-2010-congruence/) — shows PR's congruence advantage depends on which representational stage (legislature vs. government) is measured
- [Gallego, Rico & Anduiza (2012)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/gallego-rico-anduiza-2012-disproportionality/) — shows the PR/disproportionality–turnout link is conditional on democratic maturity
- [Lijphart (1999/2012)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/lijphart-patterns-of-democracy/) — anchors PR within the consensus-democracy model and links it to turnout and representation
- [Baujard et al. (2014)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/baujard-evaluative-voting-2014/) — compares approval/evaluative rules with plurality/runoff on which candidates win
- [Burnett & Kogan (2015)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/burnett-kogan-2015-ballot-exhaustion/) — measures ballot exhaustion under RCV (9.6–27.1%), so winners can take office without a majority of ballots cast
- [Neely & McDaniel (2015)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/neely-mcdaniel-2015-overvoting-equality-of-voice/) — tests whether RCV erodes equality of voice through overvoting in San Francisco
- [McDaniel (2016)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/mcdaniel-2016-writing-the-rules-racial-turnout/) — SF evidence that RCV adoption coincided with turnout declines, unevenly across racial groups
- [Donovan, Tolbert & Gracey (2016)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/donovan-tolbert-gracey-2016-campaign-civility/) — evidence that RCV cities report more civil, more satisfying campaigns
- [Cox, Fiva & Smith (2016)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/cox-fiva-smith-2016-contraction-effect/) — causal evidence that switching to PR redistributes rather than uniformly increases turnout
- [Nielson (2017)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/nielson-2017-rcv-attitudes-democracy/) — survey experiment finding RCV did not improve democratic confidence or preference for the system
- [John, Smith & Zack (2018)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/john-smith-zack-2018-alternative-vote-representation/) — tests whether RCV improves descriptive representation across 11 California cities (modest effects)
- [Juelich & Coll (2021)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/juelich-coll-2021-rcv-youth-turnout/) — evidence that RCV is associated with higher youth turnout, via candidate contact
- [FairVote (report)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/fairvote-two-decades-rcv-california/) — Bay-Area-specific descriptive evidence on RCV's turnout and representation effects across Oakland, Berkeley, San Leandro, and SF
- [Dowling et al. (2024)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/dowling-2024-rcv-turnout-mobilization/) — evidence that adopting RCV is associated with higher municipal turnout and more campaign contact
- [Dowling et al. (2025)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/dowling-2025-rcv-turnout-race-ethnicity/) — addresses whether RCV suppresses turnout among communities of color (it does not)
- [Pettigrew & Radley (2025)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/pettigrew-radley-2025-mismarked-rcv-ballots/) — documents ballot-marking errors and rejected votes as a representation cost of RCV, concentrated among marginalized voters
