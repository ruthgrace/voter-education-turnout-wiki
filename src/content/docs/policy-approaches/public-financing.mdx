**Public campaign financing** is a step removed from the ballot, but it belongs among policy
approaches because it targets *whose preferences shape the choices voters get* — who can afford
to run, and whose small donations fund them. Two designs dominate the recent evidence:
**matching funds**, where public dollars multiply small private contributions (San Francisco
runs a 6-to-1 match up to \$150), and **"democracy vouchers,"** where the government mails
residents publicly funded vouchers to give to candidates (Seattle's flagship program, often
mislabeled as San Francisco's). The animating hope is that if small and low-income donors
count for more, the donor pool — and eventually the candidate pool and the policy agenda — will
look more like the electorate.

The Seattle voucher literature is now mature and **genuinely contested**, which is why it is
worth including in full. On the optimistic side, the program sharply expanded participation:
unique donors rose roughly 350% and candidate entry ~86%, while weakening incumbents
([Griffith & Noonen](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/griffith-noonen-2022-public-campaign-funding/));
voucher users skewed lower-income than cash donors
([McCabe & Heerwig](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/mccabe-heerwig-2019-diversifying-donor-pool/));
and turnout rose an estimated ~4.9 points
([Papich](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/papich-2024-democracy-vouchers-turnout/)). On the
skeptical side, an APSR study using nine cycles of panel data finds the newly mobilized donors
mostly came from groups *already* overrepresented, so vouchers "do not systematically improve
the diversity of the donor pool"
([Yorgason](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/yorgason-2024-vouchers-donor-diversity/)), and a
field experiment shows outreach to boost voucher use worked best on frequent voters — the
people least in need of it
([Henderson & Han](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/henderson-han-2020-mobilization-vouchers/)).

The honest synthesis: public financing **broadens** participation more clearly than it
**equalizes** it. It reliably pulls in more, smaller, and somewhat less affluent donors and
more candidates, but the marginal new participant still tends to resemble the already-engaged
unless the program is paired with serious outreach to infrequent participants. San Francisco's
own matching-funds program shows the design working mechanically — public funds rose from ~33%
to ~56% of candidate spending after the match was increased
([SF Ethics Commission](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/sf-ethics-2025-public-financing-report/)) —
but its distributional effects have not been studied as rigorously as Seattle's.

## Background

| Design | How it works | Best-evidenced effect | Contested |
|---|---|---|---|
| Matching funds (e.g., SF 6:1) | Public dollars multiply small gifts | Shifts spending toward small/public dollars | Distributional/turnout effects less studied |
| Democracy vouchers (Seattle) | Residents mailed vouchers to donate | More donors, more candidates, ~+4.9 pt turnout | Whether it *diversifies* donors |

## Key Arguments

- **For:** Amplifies small and low-income donors, expands the candidate pool, weakens incumbency, and can modestly raise turnout.
- **Against / caution:** The people who take up vouchers skew toward the already-engaged, so the reform broadens participation more than it equalizes representation; effects depend heavily on outreach.
- **Relevance here:** This lever acts on *who competes and who funds them*, complementing turnout and information reforms that act on voters directly.

## Open Questions

- Can voucher/matching programs be designed (or paired with outreach) to actually reach infrequent and lower-income participants?
- Do broader donor and candidate pools translate into policy that better matches the electorate's preferences?
- How do San Francisco's matching-funds distributional effects compare with Seattle's vouchers?

## Key Research

Studies from the [Research Library](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/overview/) most relevant to this topic:

- [McCabe & Heerwig (2019)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/mccabe-heerwig-2019-diversifying-donor-pool/) — early evidence that small-donor public financing (vouchers) can broaden and diversify the campaign-donor pool
- [Henderson & Han (2020)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/henderson-han-2020-mobilization-vouchers/) — experimental evidence on which mobilization tactics raise voucher participation and for whom
- [Griffith & Noonen (2022)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/griffith-noonen-2022-public-campaign-funding/) — causal evidence that voucher financing expands the donor base and candidate entry and weakens incumbency
- [Papich (2024)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/papich-2024-democracy-vouchers-turnout/) — direct estimate that a public-financing program raises voter turnout (~4.9 points)
- [Yorgason (2024)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/yorgason-2024-vouchers-donor-diversity/) — the skeptical case that voucher financing fails to make the donor pool more representative
- [Heerwig & McCabe (2024)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/heerwig-mccabe-2024-democracy-vouchers-book/) — comprehensive assessment of small-donor/voucher public financing as a policy to improve representation
- [SF Ethics Commission (2025)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/sf-ethics-2025-public-financing-report/) — SF-specific counterpart to the Seattle voucher literature — documents the matching-funds design and its effect on small-donor reliance
