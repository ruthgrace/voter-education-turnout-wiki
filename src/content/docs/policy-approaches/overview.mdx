Policy approaches are the *structural* levers governments pull to influence who votes and
how informed those votes are. Unlike interventions — the targeted, campaign-style tactics
covered elsewhere in this wiki — policies operate on the whole electorate at once by
changing the default rules of the game: who is registered, how easy it is to cast a
ballot, what civic knowledge citizens acquire in school, and whether voting is optional at
all. Because they are durable and universal, policies can produce larger aggregate effects
than any single intervention, but they are also slower to adopt and harder to evaluate
cleanly.

A recurring theme in the research is that most turnout-boosting policies act on the *cost*
side of the voting decision rather than the *knowledge* side. Automatic registration and
vote-by-mail lower the friction of participating; they do little on their own to make
voters more informed. Civic-education mandates and, indirectly, compulsory voting are the
main policy tools aimed squarely at the education-and-quality question. This distinction
matters for the wiki's central concern — how well votes match voter preferences — because
a policy that adds many low-information voters could, in principle, weaken the link between
votes and considered preferences even as it strengthens the link between the electorate and
the population.

The comparison below situates the major policy families along two axes: their expected
effect on raw turnout and their expected effect on the informational quality or
representativeness of the votes cast. The individual pages in this section examine each in
detail, including the strength of the evidence and the trade-offs involved.

## Comparing Policy Families

| Policy | Primary lever | **Turnout effect** | Effect on informed/representative voting | Evidence strength |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Automatic voter registration | Lowers registration cost | **Small–moderate** | Neutral to slightly diluting | Strong (natural experiments) |
| Vote-by-mail & ballot access | Lowers voting cost | **Small, context-dependent** | Mostly neutral | Moderate–strong |
| Civic-education mandates | Raises knowledge | **Small, delayed** | Positive (targets quality) | Weak–moderate |
| Compulsory voting | Removes the choice not to vote | **Large** | Contested (see debate) | Strong on turnout |

## What the Research Emphasizes

- **Cost vs. knowledge.** Most adopted policies reduce the cost of voting; far fewer raise the knowledge of voters.
- **Ceilings and floors.** Access reforms tend to help most where baseline turnout is low and least where it is already high.
- **Interaction effects.** Policies and interventions compound — mail campaigns work differently under automatic registration than under opt-in systems.

## Open Questions

- Do access-expanding policies change *who* votes enough to shift *what* wins?
- Can any structural policy raise the informational quality of votes, or is that only achievable through targeted education?
- How much of a policy's measured effect is durable versus a one-time adjustment to a new default?

## Key Research

Studies from the [Research Library](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/overview/) most relevant to this topic:

- [Vanderleeuw & Engstrom (1987)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/vanderleeuw-engstrom-1987-race-referendums-rolloff/) — establishes referendums/propositions as the highest-roll-off ballot items
- [Lupia (1994)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/lupia-1994-shortcuts-encyclopedias/) — implies information environments, not just voter knowledge, shape vote quality
- [Nichols & Strizek (1995)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/nichols-strizek-1995-voting-machines-rolloff/) — shows an administrative lever (ballot technology that prompts voters about omissions) measurably raises ballot completion
- [Lijphart (1997)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/lijphart-1997-unequal-participation/) — establishes turnout inequality as the central rationale for pro-turnout institutional reform
- [Lupia & McCubbins (1998)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/lupia-mccubbins-1998-democratic-dilemma/) — draws institutional-design implications for structuring trustworthy information sources
- [Wand et al. (2001)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/wand-2001-butterfly-ballot/) — motivates ballot-design and usability standards in election administration
- [Amy (2002)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/amy-2002-real-choices-new-voices/) — frames the representation-vs-winner-take-all debate in the American context
- [Highton (2004)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/highton-2004-voter-registration-and-turnout/) — historical framing of registration as a turnout barrier
- [Hajnal & Trounstine (2005)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/hajnal-trounstine-2005-where-turnout-matters/) — motivates reforms addressing turnout inequality in local elections
- [Kimball & Kropf (2005)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/kimball-kropf-2005-ballot-design/) — directly links measurable design choices to lost/unrecorded votes, guiding ballot-design standards
- [Feig (2007)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/feig-2007-race-rolloff-straight-ticket/) — links a specific ballot-design feature to completion, showing design choices move roll-off
- [Kousser & Mullin (2007)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/kousser-mullin-2007-voting-by-mail/) — demonstrates matching on a natural experiment as a credible design for evaluating convenience-voting reforms
- [Chong & Olivera (2008)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/chong-olivera-2008-compulsory-voting-equalize-incomes/) — connects turnout inequality to policy/economic outcomes
- [Gronke et al. (2008)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/gronke-2008-convenience-voting/) — framework for classifying ballot-access reforms
- [Ho & Imai (2008)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/ho-imai-2008-ballot-order/) — rigorous evidence supporting randomized ballot ordering as an administrative safeguard
- [Selb (2008)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/selb-2008-supersized-votes-ballot-length/) — cross-national confirmation that the number of measures on a ballot depresses per-measure completion
- [Hansford & Gomez (2010)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/hansford-gomez-2010-electoral-effects-turnout/) — rigorous causal estimate of how turnout variation reshapes U.S. electoral outcomes
- [Berry & Gersen (2010)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/berry-gersen-2010-timing-of-elections/) — frames election timing as a first-order institutional lever over turnout composition, motivating consolidation reforms
- [Anzia (2011)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/anzia-2011-election-timing-interest-groups/) — foundational causal argument that on-cycle consolidation (as in CA's SB 415) reshapes both turnout and policy outcomes
- [Herrnson, Hanmer & Niemi (2012)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/herrnson-2012-ballot-type-voter-errors/) — experimental evidence on which ballot formats minimize voter error
- [Fowler (2013)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/fowler-2013-electoral-policy-consequences-turnout/) — demonstrates turnout affects downstream policy (pension spending), not just vote shares
- [Jaitman (2013)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/jaitman-2013-causal-effect-compulsory-voting/) — quantifies compulsory voting's effect on turnout inequality
- [Martin & Claibourn (2013)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/martin-claibourn-2013-citizen-participation-responsiveness/) — evidence that turnout levels causally shape legislative responsiveness and representation
- [Meredith & Salant (2013)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/meredith-salant-2013-ballot-order-effects/) — informs ballot-rotation policy to neutralize position advantage
- [Ferwerda (2014)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/ferwerda-2014-declining-participation-austria/) — tempers expectations that higher turnout reliably changes representation
- [Anzia (2014)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/anzia-2014-timing-and-turnout/) — comprehensive case that moving elections on-cycle (SB 415-style consolidation) raises turnout and broadens representation
- [Boudreau, Elmendorf & MacKenzie (2015)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/boudreau-elmendorf-mackenzie-2015-informing-electorates-election-law/) — frames the provision of political information as a design choice governable through election law
- [Burnett & Kogan (2015)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/burnett-kogan-2015-ballot-exhaustion/) — direct evidence on how RCV mechanics translate turnout into (partial) representation of preferences
- [Bechtel, Hangartner & Schmid (2016)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/bechtel-hangartner-schmid-2016-leftist-policy/) — links turnout inequality to substantive policy representation
- [Bhatti, Hansen & Wass (2016)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/bhatti-hansen-wass-2016-first-time-boost/) — informs voting-age and timing-of-first-vote policy debates
- [Augenblick & Nicholson (2016)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/augenblick-nicholson-2016-choice-fatigue/) — implies ballot length/design mechanically depresses completion on down-ballot and proposition items
- [McDaniel (2016)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/mcdaniel-2016-writing-the-rules-racial-turnout/) — direct SF evidence that RCV adoption can lower turnout overall
- [Donovan, Tolbert & Gracey (2016)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/donovan-tolbert-gracey-2016-campaign-civility/) — evidence on RCV's effects on the voter experience and campaign tone
- [Hoffman, León & Lombardi (2017)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/hoffman-leon-lombardi-2017-compulsory-voting-austria/) — evidence that higher turnout does not always translate into measurable policy change
- [Barber et al. (2017)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/barber-2017-status-quo-bias-ballot-wording/) — argues neutral, standardized ballot-measure wording matters for fair outcomes
- [Nielson (2017)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/nielson-2017-rcv-attitudes-democracy/) — experimental evidence tempering claims that RCV improves voter satisfaction
- [John, Smith & Zack (2018)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/john-smith-zack-2018-alternative-vote-representation/) — Bay Area/California evidence on whether RCV changes who gets elected
- [Yu (2019)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/yu-2019-online-voter-registration-turnout/) — quantifies a registration-access reform relevant to California's registration-modernization agenda
- [McGhee, Hill & Romero (2021)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/mcghee-hill-romero-2021-registration-turnout-effects-of-avr/) — shows reform-design details drive turnout outcomes
- [Juelich & Coll (2021)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/juelich-coll-2021-rcv-youth-turnout/) — evidence that RCV can act as a mobilizing force for younger voters
- [CIRCLE (2022)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/circle-2022-growing-voters/) — ecosystem framework spanning schools, community, and election administration
- [Hajnal, Kogan & Markarian (2022)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/hajnal-kogan-markarian-2022-who-votes/) — direct California evidence that on-cycle consolidation (SB 415) broadens and diversifies the electorate
- [Griffith & Noonen (2022)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/griffith-noonen-2022-public-campaign-funding/) — quantifies participation effects of a public-financing reform
- [FairVote (report)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/fairvote-two-decades-rcv-california/) — collates California local RCV participation and representation outcomes
- [Heerwig & McCabe (2024)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/heerwig-mccabe-2024-democracy-vouchers-book/) — synthesizes participation and candidate-pool effects of the flagship voucher program
- [Hajnal, Kogan & Markarian (2025)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/hajnal-kogan-markarian-2025-who-wins-when/) — links consolidation reforms to downstream descriptive-representation gains, not just turnout
