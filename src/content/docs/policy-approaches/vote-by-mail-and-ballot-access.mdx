**Convenience voting** reforms — no-excuse absentee ballots, universal vote-by-mail (VBM),
early in-person voting, extended hours, and making Election Day a holiday — all lower the
*cost* of casting a ballot once someone is registered and decided. They are among the most
politically visible policy levers and are frequently promoted as turnout boosters. The
research picture is more sober than the rhetoric: these reforms produce real but generally
modest turnout effects, and much of their impact is *convenience conversion* — existing
voters shifting when and how they vote — rather than mobilization of new voters.

The strongest evidence comes from staggered U.S. rollouts, especially Oregon, Washington,
Colorado, and Utah, which moved to all-mail elections. These studies typically find turnout
gains in the low single digits, with larger effects in lower-salience elections (local,
primary, midterm) than in high-salience presidential contests where motivation is already
high. Early voting alone has sometimes shown *null or even slightly negative* effects when
it disperses the mobilizing energy of a single Election Day, a counterintuitive finding
that underscores how these reforms interact with campaign behavior. VBM's clearest effects
appear among older voters and, in some studies, in raising *retention* of habitual voters
rather than recruiting new ones.

For this wiki's central concern, convenience reforms are close to neutral on the
knowledge/quality axis: they change *how* and *when* people vote, not *how informed* they
are. Their compositional effects on the electorate are generally small, which is why claims
that VBM systematically advantages one party have not held up well in the peer-reviewed
literature — a point developed further in the debate on partisan effects.

## Background

| Reform | Cost lowered | Typical turnout effect | Who benefits most |
|---|---|---|---|
| No-excuse absentee | Travel/time on Election Day | Small | Older, less mobile voters |
| Universal vote-by-mail | Travel + timing | +2–4 pts (varies) | Broad; larger in low-salience races |
| Early in-person voting | Timing rigidity | Small; sometimes null | Convenience switchers |
| Election Day holiday | Work conflict | Uncertain; small | Hourly/shift workers (in theory) |

## Key Arguments

- **For:** Lowering costs is a low-controversy way to raise participation and reduce Election Day congestion and errors.
- **Against:** Effects are modest, much of the "gain" is convenience conversion, and administration/security must scale up.
- **Design dependence:** Bundled reforms (mail + early + registration) outperform any single change in isolation.

## Open Questions

- How much convenience-reform "turnout" is genuinely new participation versus timing shifts?
- Why does early voting sometimes fail to raise, or even lower, turnout?
- Do these reforms change electoral outcomes at all, or only the logistics of voting?

## Key Research

Studies from the [Research Library](/research/overview/) most relevant to this topic:

- [Gronke et al. (2008)](/research/gronke-2008-convenience-voting/) — review of how convenience-voting modes affect turnout
- [Hansford & Gomez (2010)](/research/hansford-gomez-2010-electoral-effects-turnout/) — informs debate on whether turnout-raising reforms carry partisan consequences
- [Leighley & Nagler (2013)](/research/leighley-nagler-2013-who-votes-now/) — assesses absentee/early voting effects on turnout
- [Burden et al. (2014)](/research/burden-2014-election-laws-mobilization-turnout/) — early voting alone can reduce turnout absent complementary reforms
- [Holbein & Hillygus (2016)](/research/holbein-hillygus-2016-preregistration-youth-turnout/) — treats registration access as a lever on participation
- [Thompson et al. (2020)](/research/thompson-2020-universal-vote-by-mail-no-partisan-effect/) — causal evidence that universal VBM raises turnout slightly without changing who wins
