If most turnout policies lower the *cost* of voting, **civic-education mandates** are the
primary policy aimed at raising the *capacity* to vote well. These are requirements — set at
the state, provincial, or national level — that students complete coursework in government,
civics, or citizenship, sometimes paired with a civics assessment as a graduation
condition. The theory of change is long-horizon: instill knowledge, efficacy, and habits in
adolescence that translate into informed, durable participation in adulthood. This makes
civic education uniquely relevant to the wiki's question of whether votes match preferences,
because it targets the informational quality of the vote rather than merely its occurrence.

The evidence is genuinely mixed and weaker than for cost-lowering policies, largely because
the outcome (adult voting) is separated from the treatment (adolescent schooling) by years
and by many confounders. Traditional "seat-time" civics courses show modest and
inconsistent effects on later turnout. However, research on *active* civic learning —
simulations, service learning, discussion of controversial issues, and hands-on projects
like mock elections or actual registration drives — finds more encouraging effects on
political knowledge, efficacy, and in some studies later participation. A well-known finding
is that requiring a civics *test* to graduate raises measured knowledge but has little
demonstrated effect on turnout, illustrating that knowledge and participation are not the
same lever.

The policy debate therefore centers less on whether civic education is valuable in itself
and more on whether it is an effective *turnout* tool relative to its cost and time. Even
skeptics of its turnout effects often defend mandates on the grounds that informed
citizenship is a legitimate end in itself and that better-informed voters produce a tighter
match between votes and considered preferences — the quality argument rather than the
quantity argument.

## Background

| Approach | Format | Knowledge effect | Turnout effect | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Traditional civics course | Lecture / seat-time | Small–moderate | Weak/inconsistent | Most common mandate |
| Civics graduation test | Assessment gate | Positive on knowledge | Little evidence | Popular but blunt |
| Active/experiential civics | Simulations, projects | Moderate | Promising but under-powered | Harder to scale |
| Service / action civics | Community engagement | Moderate on efficacy | Mixed | Effects may be indirect |

## Key Arguments

- **For:** Education is the only lever that plausibly improves the *quality* of votes, not just the count; effects, though small, are durable and compound over a lifetime.
- **Against:** Effects on turnout are hard to detect, delayed, and confounded; scarce school time competes with other priorities.
- **Design matters:** Passive instruction underperforms active, participatory approaches — the format may matter more than the mandate.

## Open Questions

- Do civic-education effects persist into midlife, or fade after the first eligible election?
- Which components (knowledge, efficacy, discussion, action) actually drive later participation?
- Can civic education move the *match between votes and preferences*, or only raw knowledge scores?
