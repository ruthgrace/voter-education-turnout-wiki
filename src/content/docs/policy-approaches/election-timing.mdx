**When** an election is held turns out to be one of the most powerful levers over **who
votes** — often larger than any convenience reform. Many local elections in the United States
are held **off-cycle**: on their own date, separate from the high-salience presidential or
midterm contests. Moving them **on-cycle** — consolidating them onto the November
even-year ballot — is a structural change that costs little and, on the evidence, does more to
raise and equalize turnout than vote-by-mail or most registration reforms. This makes election
timing a first-order policy approach, and one especially relevant in California, where
**SB 415 (the California Voter Participation Rights Act)** forced many cities to consolidate
local elections onto even-year dates.

The mechanism is straightforward. Off-cycle elections raise the cost of voting: citizens must
notice a separate election, learn the low-information down-ballot contests, and turn out again.
The result is not just *lower* turnout but a *skewed* electorate — the people who still show up
are older, wealthier, more partisan, and more organized. This is why timing has a
**representation** dimension as sharp as its turnout dimension. Off-cycle scheduling
concentrates influence in **organized interest groups** whose members vote regardless of
timing: [Anzia (2011)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/anzia-2011-election-timing-interest-groups/)
shows off-cycle school districts pay experienced teachers over 3% more, and her book
[Timing and Turnout (2014)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/anzia-2014-timing-and-turnout/)
generalizes the "who shows up when nobody's watching" logic, which
[Berry & Gersen (2010)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/berry-gersen-2010-timing-of-elections/)
frame as *selective participation*.

The California consolidation experience provides unusually clean causal evidence.
[Hajnal, Kogan & Markarian (2022)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/hajnal-kogan-markarian-2022-who-votes/)
find that moving city elections on-cycle **roughly doubles turnout** and makes the electorate
markedly more representative by race, age, and partisanship — the over-55 share of local
voters fell from about half to 28%. Their follow-up
([2025](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/hajnal-kogan-markarian-2025-who-wins-when/))
shows this changes *who wins*, increasing the election of candidates of color and shrinking
Latino underrepresentation. Because these compositional shifts have predictable partisan and
group consequences, the choice of election date is itself politically contested — the debate is
not really about turnout in the abstract but about whose influence consolidation dilutes.

## Background

| Timing | Typical local turnout | Electorate composition | Who benefits |
|---|---|---|---|
| Off-cycle (standalone date) | Low (often 15–30%) | Older, wealthier, more organized | Organized interest groups, incumbents |
| On-cycle (with midterms) | Higher | Broader | Broader electorate |
| On-cycle (with presidential) | Highest (~2× off-cycle) | Most representative by race/age/party | Broad electorate; candidates of color |

## Key Arguments

- **For consolidation:** Cheap, durable, and delivers the largest turnout and representativeness gains of any timing/access reform; reduces the leverage of narrow organized interests.
- **Against / cautions:** Longer ballots raise **roll-off** and choice fatigue on the added local contests (see [Voter Guides and Information](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/interventions/voter-guides-and-information/)); local issues may get less attention buried under national races.
- **Political reality:** Groups advantaged by low off-cycle turnout resist consolidation, so timing rules are fought over as hard as the outcomes they produce.

## Open Questions

- How much of the newly added on-cycle vote is *informed* about local contests versus rolled-off or party-line? (The turnout gain is clear; the vote-quality effect is not.)
- Does consolidation's representational benefit persist, or do organized groups adapt their strategies?
- Can pairing consolidation with plain-language local voter guides capture the turnout gain without a roll-off penalty?

## Key Research

Studies from the [Research Library](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/overview/) most relevant to this topic:

- [Berry & Gersen (2010)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/berry-gersen-2010-timing-of-elections/) — theorizes "selective participation" — decoupling local from federal elections raises the cost of voting so only high-stakes voters turn out
- [Anzia (2011)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/anzia-2011-election-timing-interest-groups/) — foundational causal evidence that off-cycle timing depresses turnout and shifts policy toward organized groups
- [Anzia (2014)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/anzia-2014-timing-and-turnout/) — book-length case that off-cycle elections favor organized groups and that on-cycle consolidation broadens the electorate
- [Hajnal, Kogan & Markarian (2022)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/hajnal-kogan-markarian-2022-who-votes/) — direct California evidence that on-cycle consolidation roughly doubles turnout and diversifies the electorate
- [Hajnal, Kogan & Markarian (2025)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/hajnal-kogan-markarian-2025-who-wins-when/) — shows on-cycle timing changes who wins, increasing the election of candidates of color
