**Compulsory voting** makes participation a legal obligation, typically enforced with a
modest fine, and it is by a wide margin the most powerful turnout lever any government has.
Countries with enforced mandates — Australia is the canonical case, with Belgium and several
Latin American states as others — routinely post turnout in the high 80s to 90s percent,
20–30 points above comparable voluntary systems. Because the policy removes the choice not
to vote rather than lowering a cost or raising knowledge, it is analytically distinct from
every other approach in this section and is treated at length in the Key Debates section
under [voluntary vs. mandatory voting](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/key-debates/voluntary-vs-mandatory/).

What makes compulsory voting central to this wiki is precisely its tension between the two
outcomes we care about. On representativeness, the case is strong: near-universal turnout
eliminates the class, age, and income skew that plagues voluntary systems, so the voting
electorate closely mirrors the citizenry and outcomes should better track the population's
preferences. On informational quality, the case is contested: mandates pull in citizens who
would not otherwise vote and who are, on average, less politically informed, raising the
worry of "random" or low-information ballots, higher rates of donkey voting and spoiled
ballots, and choices driven by ballot position rather than preference.

The empirical evidence resolves some of this. Turnout effects are large and robust.
Effects on *policy outcomes* are real but smaller than theory predicts — studies of
Australia and of natural experiments elsewhere find compulsory voting shifts spending and
party support modestly toward lower-income preferences, consistent with the
representativeness argument. Evidence that compelled voters cast meaningfully "worse" votes
is weaker than critics assume; many compensate by acquiring just enough information, and
spoiled-ballot rates, while elevated, remain a small share of the total.

## Background

| Dimension | Voluntary systems | Compulsory (enforced) |
|---|---|---|
| Typical turnout | 50–70% | 85–95% |
| Turnout inequality by income/age | High | Low |
| Informational quality of marginal voter | Higher (self-selected) | Lower on average |
| Spoiled/blank ballots | Low | Somewhat higher |
| Policy tilt | Toward high-propensity groups | Slightly toward broader electorate |

## Key Arguments

- **For:** Delivers representativeness no other policy can; a near-full electorate is the tightest possible match between voters and the population.
- **Against:** Coerces the uninterested into voting, potentially adding low-information noise and raising legitimacy objections to compulsion itself.
- **Middle ground:** Enforcement intensity matters — light-touch mandates capture most of the turnout gain with less coercion.

## Open Questions

- Does the added turnout improve the match between outcomes and preferences, or add noise that cancels out?
- How much do compelled voters invest in becoming informed once voting is unavoidable?
- Are the modest policy shifts observed under compulsion durable or transitional?

## Key Research

Studies from the [Research Library](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/overview/) most relevant to this topic:

- [Lijphart (1997)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/lijphart-1997-unequal-participation/) — canonical argument that mandatory voting equalizes participation and political influence
- [Chong & Olivera (2008)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/chong-olivera-2008-compulsory-voting-equalize-incomes/) — cross-national evidence linking enforced compulsory voting to lower income inequality
- [Fowler (2013)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/fowler-2013-electoral-policy-consequences-turnout/) — causal estimate that compulsory voting sharply raises turnout and reshapes electoral outcomes
- [Jaitman (2013)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/jaitman-2013-causal-effect-compulsory-voting/) — causal evidence that compulsion raises turnout and disproportionately mobilizes lower-skill citizens
- [Brennan & Hill (2014)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/brennan-hill-2014-compulsory-voting-for-and-against/) — comprehensive normative and empirical assessment of the policy
- [Ferwerda (2014)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/ferwerda-2014-declining-participation-austria/) — estimates electoral effects of abolishing compulsory voting
- [Bechtel, Hangartner & Schmid (2016)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/bechtel-hangartner-schmid-2016-leftist-policy/) — quasi-experimental evidence that mandatory voting boosts turnout and shifts referendum outcomes leftward
- [Miller & Dassonneville (2016)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/miller-dassonneville-2016-high-turnout-low-countries/) — evidence on electoral consequences of removing a compulsory-voting regime
- [Hoffman, León & Lombardi (2017)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/hoffman-leon-lombardi-2017-compulsory-voting-austria/) — clean causal estimate of compulsory voting's turnout effect in a developed democracy
- [Singh (2021)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/singh-2021-beyond-turnout/) — synthesizes evidence on how compulsion changes voters and parties beyond raising turnout
