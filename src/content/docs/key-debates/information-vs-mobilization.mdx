This debate cuts to the heart of the wiki's title. When an intervention "works," *what* is
it actually doing? The **mobilization** view holds that most effective tactics — canvassing,
mail, texts, social pressure — raise turnout by lowering the psychological or logistical
cost of an act people were already inclined toward; they change *whether* someone votes, not
*what they know* or *how they choose*. The **information** view holds that a distinct class
of interventions — voter guides, civics, informational outreach — works by changing what
voters know and therefore *how well their votes reflect their preferences*. The empirical
literature increasingly suggests these are **two largely separate levers**, and conflating
them is a common analytical error.

The evidence for the separation is striking. The tactics with the *largest* turnout effects
(social-pressure mail, quality canvassing) are precisely the ones whose *message content*
matters least — what moves voters is the personal ask or the social norm, not any
information conveyed. Conversely, the interventions that most improve *knowledge and vote
choice* (voting-advice applications, ballot guides, active civics) tend to have *small and
inconsistent* turnout effects and often reach the already-engaged. In other words, the
tactics that best mobilize and the tactics that best inform are mostly different tools, and a
program optimized for one is rarely optimal for the other.

This matters because it reframes the entire policy question. If the goal is *turnout*,
mobilization tactics dominate and information is nearly beside the point. If the goal is
votes that *better match preferences*, mobilization does little and the weaker,
harder-to-scale information tools are where the leverage lies. Much public rhetoric assumes
"educating voters" will both raise turnout and improve choices; the research suggests it
usually does neither strongly, and that treating "voter education" as a turnout strategy is
a category error.

## Background

| Dimension | Mobilization lever | Information lever |
|---|---|---|
| Core mechanism | Lower cost / social norm / reminder | Raise knowledge / reduce uncertainty |
| Example tactics | Canvassing, mail, texts, social pressure | Voter guides, VAAs, civics |
| Effect on turnout | Often large | Small, inconsistent |
| Effect on vote choice / quality | Minimal | Measurable |
| Whom it reaches | Marginal, low-propensity voters | Already-engaged voters |

## Key Arguments

- **Mobilization primacy:** The best-evidenced, highest-effect tactics work through contact and norms, not information — content barely matters.
- **Information primacy:** Only informational tools address vote *quality*; turnout without informed choice is a hollow metric.
- **Synthesis:** They are complementary, not competing — but each requires its own tactics, metrics, and success criteria.

## Open Questions

- Can a single program meaningfully both mobilize the disengaged *and* inform their choices?
- How should success be measured when turnout and vote quality diverge?
- Does mobilizing low-information voters improve representation or merely add noise? (See the [informed-voting](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/key-debates/informed-voting-vs-turnout/) debate.)

## Key Research

Studies from the [Research Library](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/overview/) most relevant to this topic:

- [Converse (1990)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/converse-1990-popular-representation-information/) — grounds whether individual-level information matters given aggregation
- [Page & Shapiro (1992)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/page-shapiro-1992-rational-public/) — foundational reference for whether informing individuals matters at the aggregate level
- [Lau & Redlawsk (1997)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/lau-redlawsk-1997-voting-correctly/) — reframes democratic competence around decision quality rather than knowledge quantity
- [Gerber & Green (2000)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/gerber-green-2000-canvassing-phone-mail/) — demonstrates turnout gains from mobilization independent of new political information
- [Gerber, Green & Shachar (2003)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/gerber-green-shachar-2003-voting-habit-forming/) — foundational habit-formation result underpinning why early mobilization compounds over time
- [Lassen (2005)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/lassen-2005-information-voter-turnout/) — causal evidence that information itself raises turnout, not just vote choice
- [Nickerson (2006)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/nickerson-2006-volunteer-phone-calls/) — shows the personal touch, not information content, drives phone-call effectiveness
- [Nickerson (2007)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/nickerson-2007-quality-is-job-one-phone/) — reinforces that manner of contact, not information, produces mobilization
- [Larcinese (2007)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/larcinese-2007-political-knowledge-turnout/) — quantifies political knowledge as a major driver of turnout
- [Gerber, Green & Larimer (2008)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/gerber-green-larimer-2008-social-pressure/) — evidence that normative/social incentives, not information, drive the effect
- [Dale & Strauss (2009)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/dale-strauss-2009-text-message-reminders/) — supports a reminder/salience mechanism distinct from personal social connection
- [Malhotra et al. (2011)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/malhotra-michelson-rogers-valenzuela-2011-text-messages/) — evidence that a noticeable prompt, not social connection or information, can suffice
- [Bond et al. (2012)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/bond-2012-61-million-person-experiment/) — evidence that mobilization framing plus social cues, not information alone, drives turnout
- [Garzia & Marschall (2012)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/garzia-marschall-2012-vaa-state-of-research/) — surveys VAA effects on both informing voters and mobilizing turnout
- [Broockman & Green (2014)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/broockman-green-2014-online-advertisements-name-recognition/) — shows digital ad exposure did not translate into changed candidate evaluations
- [Gemenis & Rosema (2014)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/gemenis-rosema-2014-vaa-electoral-turnout/) — shows an information intervention can double as a mobilization tool
- [Burden et al. (2014)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/burden-2014-election-laws-mobilization-turnout/) — reforms interact with campaign mobilization, sometimes perversely
- [Nickerson (2015)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/nickerson-2015-voter-registration-drives/) — mobilizing registration produces net new participation
- [Broockman & Kalla (2016)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/broockman-kalla-2016-durably-reducing-transphobia/) — demonstrates persuasion (attitude change) is achievable through interpersonal, non-argumentative contact
- [Bhatti, Hansen & Wass (2016)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/bhatti-hansen-wass-2016-first-time-boost/) — social embeddedness at the first election as a mobilization mechanism for habit formation
- [Neundorf, Niemi & Smets (2016)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/neundorf-niemi-smets-2016-compensation-effect-civic-education/) — civic education as a compensatory socialization channel
- [Achen & Bartels (2016)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/achen-bartels-2016-democracy-for-realists/) — questions whether informing voters meaningfully changes outcomes
- [Jones et al. (2017)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/jones-2017-social-influence-2012-election/) — reinforces that social mobilization cues, not information, produce the effect
- [Kalla & Broockman (2018)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/kalla-broockman-2018-minimal-persuasive-effects/) — cornerstone "minimal effects" statement distinguishing persuasion from mobilization
- [Coppock, Hill & Vavreck (2020)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/coppock-hill-vavreck-2020-small-effects-advertising/) — supports the minimal-persuasion view of campaign messaging
- [Kalla & Broockman (2020)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/kalla-broockman-2020-reducing-exclusionary-attitudes/) — argument-only (informational) contact failed while perspective-taking succeeded
- [Holbein & Hillygus (2020)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/holbein-hillygus-2020-making-young-voters/) — locates the youth problem in follow-through, not information or interest
- [Boudreau, Colner & MacKenzie (2021)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/boudreau-colner-mackenzie-2021-voter-guides-narrow-gap/) — an information intervention that changes the quality and expression of votes rather than turnout
- [Coppock, Green & Porter (2022)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/coppock-green-porter-2022-digital-advertising-vote-choice/) — evidence that digital ad exposure does not shift electoral outcomes
- [Aggarwal et al. (2023)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/aggarwal-2023-campaign-wide-digital-advertising-turnout/) — digital advertising did not mobilize turnout at scale
