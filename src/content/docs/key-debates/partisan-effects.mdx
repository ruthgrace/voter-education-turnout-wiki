Almost every reform and tactic in this wiki is contested less on whether it *works* than on
*whom it helps*. The **partisan-effects** debate asks whether education and turnout
interventions are politically neutral or systematically advantage one party — because that
question, more than any evidence about effectiveness, determines which reforms get adopted.
The conventional assumption, rooted in the demographics of who abstains, is that raising
turnout helps the left: non-voters skew younger, poorer, and more diverse, groups that lean
progressive in many democracies. If true, turnout-expanding reforms would be inherently
partisan, and the fierce political fights over registration, mail voting, and access would be
rational rather than performative.

The empirical literature has substantially **complicated this assumption**. A growing body
of work finds that the partisan effect of higher turnout is *small, inconsistent, and
often close to zero*. Marginal (newly mobilized) voters are less firmly attached to either
party and more responsive to short-term conditions, so they do not reliably break for the
left; several natural experiments on universal vote-by-mail, for instance, find **negligible
partisan advantage** despite raising turnout. The older "turnout helps Democrats" heuristic
appears to have been overstated — the composition of non-voters has shifted, and marginal
voters are precisely the swingy, weakly-aligned citizens least likely to move an election in
a predictable direction.

This has two important implications. First, much of the political conflict over access
reforms may be fighting over an *illusory* partisan prize — the reforms change *who votes*
more than *what wins*. Second, the neutrality question is genuinely different for
*information* interventions than for *mobilization* ones: a voting-advice tool that helps
voters find their best-matching party is "partisan" only in the sense of improving
preference-matching, whereas a targeted mobilization program is partisan by *design* when a
campaign runs it. Distinguishing *tactic* neutrality from *deployment* neutrality is
essential to keeping this debate coherent.

## Background

| Reform / tactic | Assumed partisan lean | What the evidence suggests |
|---|---|---|
| Higher turnout generally | Helps the left | Small, inconsistent; often near zero |
| Universal vote-by-mail | Contested | Little net partisan advantage |
| Automatic registration | Helps the left | Modest at most; marginal voters are swingy |
| Campaign-run mobilization | Helps the running party | Partisan by deployment, not by mechanism |
| Nonpartisan information tools | Neutral | Neutral by design; improves matching |

## Key Arguments

- **Turnout-is-partisan view:** Non-voters lean one way, so expanding turnout predictably favors that side; reforms are therefore political.
- **Neutrality view:** Marginal voters are weakly aligned and swingy; higher turnout changes who votes far more than who wins.
- **Distinction that matters:** A tactic can be neutral in mechanism yet partisan in deployment — conflating the two muddies the debate.

## Open Questions

- Under what conditions (which elections, which countries) does higher turnout actually tilt outcomes?
- Has the leftward skew of non-voters weakened as party coalitions realign by education and age?
- Should reform design deliberately aim for *demonstrable* neutrality to defuse political resistance?

## Key Research

Studies from the [Research Library](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/overview/) most relevant to this topic:

- [DeNardo (1980)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/denardo-1980-turnout-and-the-vote/) — the classic statement questioning whether higher turnout benefits the left
- [Vanderleeuw & Engstrom (1987)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/vanderleeuw-engstrom-1987-race-referendums-rolloff/) — documents that roll-off is not uniform across voter groups, raising representational-equity stakes for who completes the ballot
- [Bartels (1996)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/bartels-1996-uninformed-votes/) — shows information effects can systematically favor particular parties/candidates
- [McDermott (1998)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/mcdermott-1998-race-gender-cues/) — non-party cues (race, gender) can independently signal ideology
- [Lau & Redlawsk (2001)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/lau-redlawsk-2001-cognitive-heuristics/) — party/ideology cues have uneven effects on decision quality across voter types
- [Citrin, Schickler & Sides (2003)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/citrin-schickler-sides-2003-what-if-everyone-voted/) — simulation evidence that full turnout would rarely change who wins
- [Highton (2004)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/highton-2004-voter-registration-and-turnout/) — assesses the partisan stakes of registration reform
- [Hajnal & Trounstine (2005)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/hajnal-trounstine-2005-where-turnout-matters/) — evidence that uneven turnout biases representation (racial/ethnic composition effects)
- [Feig (2007)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/feig-2007-race-rolloff-straight-ticket/) — connects roll-off patterns to straight-ticket behavior and group differences
- [Gronke et al. (2008)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/gronke-2008-convenience-voting/) — evidence that convenience reforms mostly retain, not add, voters
- [Boudreau (2009)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/boudreau-2009-closing-the-gap/) — endorser incentives (truth-telling vs. incentive to lie) condition when cues help versus mislead
- [Hansford & Gomez (2010)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/hansford-gomez-2010-electoral-effects-turnout/) — instrumental-variable evidence on whether higher turnout benefits Democrats
- [Nyhan & Reifler (2010)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/nyhan-reifler-2010-when-corrections-fail/) — shows correction effectiveness is conditioned by ideology/partisanship
- [Bullock (2011)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/bullock-2011-elite-influence-informed-electorate/) — shows policy information rivals or exceeds party-cue influence, challenging strong elite-dominance accounts
- [Anzia (2011)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/anzia-2011-election-timing-interest-groups/) — shows low-turnout off-cycle elections systematically advantage organized interest blocs
- [Gallego, Rico & Anduiza (2012)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/gallego-rico-anduiza-2012-disproportionality/) — mechanism runs through small- vs. large-party supporter mobilization
- [Fowler (2013)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/fowler-2013-electoral-policy-consequences-turnout/) — strong evidence that higher turnout can benefit left/labor parties
- [Martin & Claibourn (2013)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/martin-claibourn-2013-citizen-participation-responsiveness/) — links participation inequality to unequal policy representation
- [Leighley & Nagler (2013)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/leighley-nagler-2013-who-votes-now/) — documents systematic preference differences between voters and nonvoters
- [Ferwerda (2014)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/ferwerda-2014-declining-participation-austria/) — natural-experiment evidence that turnout decline has minimal systematic partisan consequences
- [Boudreau & MacKenzie (2014)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/boudreau-mackenzie-2014-informing-the-electorate/) — evidence that policy information can override party endorsements
- [Anzia (2014)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/anzia-2014-timing-and-turnout/) — central evidence that election calendars, not just rules, determine which interests prevail
- [Baujard et al. (2014)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/baujard-evaluative-voting-2014/) — documents a systematic advantage shift from polarizing to consensual candidates under alternative rules
- [Hersh (2015)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/hersh-2015-hacking-the-electorate/) — shows how data availability shapes which (often partisan) voters campaigns mobilize
- [Boudreau, Elmendorf & MacKenzie (2015)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/boudreau-elmendorf-mackenzie-2015-informing-electorates-election-law/) — compares partisan endorsements against nonpartisan policy guides as competing information sources
- [Bechtel, Hangartner & Schmid (2016)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/bechtel-hangartner-schmid-2016-leftist-policy/) — shows compulsion changes policy preferences expressed at the ballot, favoring left positions
- [Miller & Dassonneville (2016)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/miller-dassonneville-2016-high-turnout-low-countries/) — quasi-experimental test of the "high turnout helps the left" claim
- [Holbein & Hillygus (2016)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/holbein-hillygus-2016-preregistration-youth-turnout/) — finds preregistration mobilizes youth without clear partisan tilt
- [Achen & Bartels (2016)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/achen-bartels-2016-democracy-for-realists/) — centers partisan/group identity as the driver of vote choice
- [McDaniel (2016)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/mcdaniel-2016-writing-the-rules-racial-turnout/) — documents unequal turnout effects across racial/ethnic groups, informing debates over who is advantaged by the reform
- [Cox, Fiva & Smith (2016)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/cox-fiva-smith-2016-contraction-effect/) — links electoral rule to parties' geographic mobilization strategies
- [Hoffman, León & Lombardi (2017)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/hoffman-leon-lombardi-2017-compulsory-voting-austria/) — tests and largely fails to find downstream fiscal/policy consequences of mandated turnout
- [Berinsky (2017)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/berinsky-2017-rumors-health-care-reform/) — demonstrates source credibility/partisanship shapes whether corrections work
- [John, Smith & Zack (2018)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/john-smith-zack-2018-alternative-vote-representation/) — tests claims that RCV boosts representation of underrepresented groups
- [Boudreau, Elmendorf & MacKenzie (2019)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/boudreau-elmendorf-mackenzie-2019-roadmaps-to-representation/) — when policy maps conflict with party cues, substantial numbers of voters override the partisan cue
- [McCabe & Heerwig (2019)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/mccabe-heerwig-2019-diversifying-donor-pool/) — documents who participates in campaign finance and how a reform shifts it
- [Coppock, Hill & Vavreck (2020)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/coppock-hill-vavreck-2020-small-effects-advertising/) — shows small effects hold across partisan senders and receivers
- [Thompson et al. (2020)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/thompson-2020-universal-vote-by-mail-no-partisan-effect/) — strong evidence that a major convenience-voting reform is partisan-neutral
- [Coppock, Green & Porter (2022)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/coppock-green-porter-2022-digital-advertising-vote-choice/) — partisan-targeted ads failed to move partisan vote share
- [CIRCLE (2022)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/circle-2022-growing-voters/) — reports civic education improves engagement without increasing partisanship
- [Grumbach & Hill (2022)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/grumbach-hill-2022-rock-the-registration/) — reforms that add young registrants can shift electorate composition
- [Hajnal, Kogan & Markarian (2022)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/hajnal-kogan-markarian-2022-who-votes/) — shows timing changes who votes, with predictable partisan and demographic consequences
- [Yorgason (2024)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/yorgason-2024-vouchers-donor-diversity/) — shows uneven uptake across partisan and demographic groups, so the reform may not neutralize participation gaps
- [Hajnal, Kogan & Markarian (2025)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/hajnal-kogan-markarian-2025-who-wins-when/) — demonstrates timing-driven electorate change translates into different electoral winners
- [Dowling et al. (2025)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/dowling-2025-rcv-turnout-race-ethnicity/) — informs the equity assessment of RCV as an electoral-system reform
