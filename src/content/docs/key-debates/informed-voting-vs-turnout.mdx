Suppose you could raise turnout by pulling many low-information citizens to the polls, or
you could improve the quality of votes without raising turnout at all. Which is the better
democratic outcome? This is the **quality-vs-quantity** debate, and it is where the two
outcomes this wiki tracks — participation and preference-matching — come into direct tension.
The **maximum-turnout** position treats participation as the paramount value: a fuller
electorate is more legitimate and more representative, and worries about "informed" voting
have historically been used to justify excluding the poor and marginalized. The
**informed-voting** position treats the *content* of votes as what matters: a vote cast
without knowledge of the choices cannot express a genuine preference, so more such votes may
add noise rather than signal.

Underneath the goal question sits a **mechanism** question that is easy to conflate with it:
do the interventions in this wiki work by *informing* voters or by *mobilizing* them? The
research increasingly treats these as **two largely separate levers**. The tactics with the
*largest turnout effects* (social-pressure mail, quality canvassing) are precisely the ones
whose *message content* matters least — what moves voters is the personal ask or the social
norm, not any information conveyed. Conversely, the tools that most improve *knowledge and
vote choice* (voting-advice applications, ballot guides, active civics) tend to have *small
and inconsistent* turnout effects. In other words, the tactics that best mobilize and the
tactics that best inform are mostly different tools — so "educating voters" and "turning them
out" are not one program but two.

The tension is real but often overstated, and the evidence points toward a partial
reconciliation. The "**miracle of aggregation**" and cue-taking literatures suggest that
uninformed voters do not necessarily vote randomly — they use heuristics (party, endorsements,
retrospective judgments) that can let them vote *as if* better informed. But low information
is *not* randomly distributed: it correlates with class, and systematic (not random) errors
do **not** cancel in aggregate, which is how uninformed voting can bias outcomes rather than
merely add noise. So whether more turnout helps or hurts depends on whether the added voters'
errors are random (harmless) or systematic (distorting) — and on whether we also lower the
cost of voting well.

## Two Levers: Informing vs. Mobilizing

Because mobilization and information are separate levers, a program optimized for one is
rarely optimal for the other. This reframes the whole debate: if the goal is *turnout*,
mobilization tactics dominate and information is nearly beside the point; if the goal is votes
that *better match preferences*, mobilization does little and the weaker, harder-to-scale
information tools are where the leverage lies.

| Dimension | Mobilization lever | Information lever |
|---|---|---|
| Core mechanism | Lower cost / social norm / reminder | Raise knowledge / reduce uncertainty |
| Example tactics | Canvassing, mail, texts, social pressure | Voter guides, VAAs, cues, civics |
| Effect on turnout | Often large | Small, inconsistent |
| Effect on vote choice / quality | Minimal | Measurable |
| Whom it reaches | Marginal, low-propensity voters | Already-engaged voters |

## What Moves Votes Toward — or Away From — Preferences

If information is the lever for *quality*, a practical question follows: what information
actually makes people vote closer to their own preferences, and what pushes them further?
The research points both ways.

**Toward preferences.** Trustworthy **cues and endorsements** can let low-information voters
mimic informed choices — Lupia's California-insurance study and Lupia & McCubbins's theory
show shortcuts can substitute for encyclopedic knowledge *when the source is knowledgeable
and trusted*. Nonpartisan **voter guides**, candidate policy statements, and spatial "maps"
of candidate positions reliably raise the match between voters' views and their choice,
with the largest gains among the *least* informed (Boudreau and colleagues).

**Away from preferences.** The same low-information conditions make votes vulnerable.
**Misinformation** — confidently held false beliefs — distorts choices, and corrections often
fail or even backfire (Kuklinski et al.; Nyhan & Reifler). Purely presentational features of
the ballot can override preference entirely: **ballot-order effects** hand first-listed
candidates several extra points (Ho & Imai; Meredith & Salant), **confusing ballot design**
caused thousands of misdirected votes in Palm Beach County and drives measurable rates of
lost or wrong-candidate votes (Wand et al.; Herrnson et al.; Kimball & Kropf), and the mere
**wording/framing** of a measure can move support independent of the underlying policy
(Barber et al.). Even demographic **cues** can mislead when voters infer positions from a
candidate's race or gender rather than their actual platform (McDermott).

## Background

| Position | Core value | Key claim | Main risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| Maximum turnout | Participation / legitimacy | Fuller electorate = more representative | Adds low-information voters |
| Informed voting | Vote quality / preference-match | Uninformed votes may distort outcomes | Slippery slope to exclusion |
| Aggregation optimist | Both | Heuristics let uninformed votes still signal | Assumes errors are random |
| Systematic-bias skeptic | Vote quality | Non-random ignorance biases outcomes | Understates heuristics |

## Key Arguments

- **For maximum turnout:** Representativeness is a democratic good in itself; "informedness" tests have an ugly exclusionary history.
- **For informed voting:** A vote without knowledge of the options cannot express a preference; systematic ignorance skews, not cancels.
- **The levers are separable:** Mobilization raises quantity, information raises quality — so pursue both by pairing turnout drives with genuine information provision rather than choosing.
- **Design the ballot, not just the voter:** Because order, layout, and wording distort votes on their own, improving the *choice environment* can raise vote quality without any change in voter knowledge.

## Open Questions

- Are the errors of newly mobilized low-information voters random (harmless) or systematic (distorting)?
- Which information actually improves preference-matching — substantive guides, trusted cues, or both — and for whom?
- Can a single program meaningfully both mobilize the disengaged *and* inform their choices?
- How much of the gap between votes and preferences is caused by the *ballot itself* rather than by voters?
- How should success be measured when turnout and vote quality diverge?

## Key Research

Studies from the [Research Library](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/overview/) most relevant to this topic:

- [Converse (1990)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/converse-1990-popular-representation-information/) — origin of the "miracle of aggregation" concept central to debates on voter competence; grounds whether individual-level information matters given aggregation
- [Page & Shapiro (1992)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/page-shapiro-1992-rational-public/) — articulates the aggregation argument that collective opinion can be competent despite individual ignorance; foundational reference for whether informing individuals matters at the aggregate level
- [Lupia (1994)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/lupia-1994-shortcuts-encyclopedias/) — central to the debate over whether encyclopedic knowledge is necessary for competent voting
- [Delli Carpini & Keeter (1996)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/delli-carpini-keeter-1996-what-americans-know/) — documents that the informed both participate more and vote more consistently with their interests
- [Bartels (1996)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/bartels-1996-uninformed-votes/) — central evidence that information changes vote choice, challenging "low-information rationality"
- [Lau & Redlawsk (1997)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/lau-redlawsk-1997-voting-correctly/) — defines the "correct voting" standard for whether votes match voters' informed preferences; reframes democratic competence around decision quality rather than knowledge quantity
- [Lupia & McCubbins (1998)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/lupia-mccubbins-1998-democratic-dilemma/) — argues limited information need not prevent reasoned, preference-congruent choices
- [McDermott (1998)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/mcdermott-1998-race-gender-cues/) — shows low-information demographic heuristics can move voters toward or away from their true preferences
- [Gerber & Green (2000)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/gerber-green-2000-canvassing-phone-mail/) — demonstrates turnout gains from mobilization independent of new political information
- [Kuklinski et al. (2000)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/kuklinski-2000-misinformation-currency/) — foundational evidence that confidently held false beliefs (not just low information) push expressed preferences away from informed ones
- [Wattenberg, McAllister & Salvanto (2000)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/wattenberg-mcallister-salvanto-2000-voting-sat-test/) — frames down-ballot abstention as an information problem: voters who "don't know" skip races even after showing up
- [Galston (2001)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/galston-2001-political-knowledge-engagement-civic-education/) — centers political knowledge as distinct from and prerequisite to informed participation
- [Lau & Redlawsk (2001)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/lau-redlawsk-2001-cognitive-heuristics/) — shows heuristics do not reliably substitute for information, especially for the less informed
- [Wand et al. (2001)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/wand-2001-butterfly-ballot/) — canonical case where ballot design alone caused votes to diverge from voters' actual intent
- [Gerber, Green & Shachar (2003)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/gerber-green-shachar-2003-voting-habit-forming/) — foundational habit-formation result underpinning why early mobilization compounds over time
- [Meirick & Wackman (2004)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/meirick-wackman-2004-kids-voting-political-knowledge/) — shows a school program improving informedness, not just turnout
- [Lassen (2005)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/lassen-2005-information-voter-turnout/) — causal evidence that information itself raises turnout, not just vote choice; connects the information environment to participation
- [Kimball & Kropf (2005)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/kimball-kropf-2005-ballot-design/) — shows usability failures cause intended votes not to be counted
- [Nickerson (2006)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/nickerson-2006-volunteer-phone-calls/) — shows the personal touch, not information content, drives phone-call effectiveness
- [Nickerson (2007)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/nickerson-2007-quality-is-job-one-phone/) — reinforces that manner of contact, not information, produces mobilization
- [Larcinese (2007)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/larcinese-2007-political-knowledge-turnout/) — quantifies political knowledge as a major driver of turnout; links knowledge to the decision to vote
- [Gerber, Green & Larimer (2008)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/gerber-green-larimer-2008-social-pressure/) — evidence that normative/social incentives, not information, drive the effect
- [Campbell (2008)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/campbell-2008-voice-in-the-classroom/) — links open discussion to intention to be an informed voter, connecting knowledge and turnout dispositions
- [Kam & Palmer (2008)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/kam-palmer-2008-reconsidering-education-participation/) — central skeptical position on whether education itself causes participation
- [Lau, Andersen & Redlawsk (2008)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/lau-andersen-redlawsk-2008-exploration-correct-voting/) — provides over-time estimates of how well votes match informed preferences
- [Ho & Imai (2008)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/ho-imai-2008-ballot-order/) — quantifies how a ballot-position artifact, not preference, shifts votes
- [Selb (2008)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/selb-2008-supersized-votes-ballot-length/) — ties measure abstention to uncertainty, suggesting information provision could partly offset length effects
- [Dale & Strauss (2009)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/dale-strauss-2009-text-message-reminders/) — supports a reminder/salience mechanism distinct from personal social connection
- [Boudreau (2009)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/boudreau-2009-closing-the-gap/) — evidence that cues can substitute for encyclopedic information in producing better-matched votes
- [Arceneaux & Kolodny (2009)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/arceneaux-kolodny-2009-educating-least-informed/) — tests the low-information-rationality claim in an actual campaign, distinguishing informational cue effects from turnout mobilization
- [Sondheimer & Green (2010)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/sondheimer-green-2010-education-turnout-experiments/) — bears on whether education's effect is real versus a proxy for background
- [Malhotra et al. (2011)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/malhotra-michelson-rogers-valenzuela-2011-text-messages/) — evidence that a noticeable prompt, not social connection or information, can suffice
- [Bullock (2011)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/bullock-2011-elite-influence-informed-electorate/) — evidence that informed citizens reason from policy, not just party
- [Reilly & Richey (2011)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/reilly-richey-2011-ballot-question-readability-rolloff/) — locates a comprehension barrier specific to ballot measures, where roll-off is worst
- [Bond et al. (2012)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/bond-2012-61-million-person-experiment/) — evidence that mobilization framing plus social cues, not information alone, drives turnout
- [Garzia & Marschall (2012)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/garzia-marschall-2012-vaa-state-of-research/) — surveys VAA effects on both informing voters and mobilizing turnout
- [Herrnson, Hanmer & Niemi (2012)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/herrnson-2012-ballot-type-voter-errors/) — isolates ballot design as a cause of votes not matching intended choices
- [Martin & Claibourn (2013)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/martin-claibourn-2013-citizen-participation-responsiveness/) — shows aggregate participation (not just informed voting) drives representational outcomes
- [Meredith & Salant (2013)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/meredith-salant-2013-ballot-order-effects/) — shows a substantial position-driven distortion of outcomes independent of voter preference
- [Broockman & Green (2014)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/broockman-green-2014-online-advertisements-name-recognition/) — shows digital ad exposure did not translate into changed candidate evaluations
- [Brennan & Hill (2014)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/brennan-hill-2014-compulsory-voting-for-and-against/) — engages whether compelling uninformed citizens to vote improves or degrades outcomes
- [Gemenis & Rosema (2014)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/gemenis-rosema-2014-vaa-electoral-turnout/) — shows an information intervention can double as a mobilization tool
- [Burden et al. (2014)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/burden-2014-election-laws-mobilization-turnout/) — reforms interact with campaign mobilization, sometimes perversely
- [Boudreau & MacKenzie (2014)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/boudreau-mackenzie-2014-informing-the-electorate/) — bears on whether voters reason substantively or defer blindly to elites
- [Nickerson (2015)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/nickerson-2015-voter-registration-drives/) — mobilizing registration produces net new participation
- [Broockman & Kalla (2016)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/broockman-kalla-2016-durably-reducing-transphobia/) — demonstrates persuasion (attitude change) is achievable through interpersonal, non-argumentative contact
- [Bhatti, Hansen & Wass (2016)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/bhatti-hansen-wass-2016-first-time-boost/) — social embeddedness at the first election as a mobilization mechanism for habit formation
- [Neundorf, Niemi & Smets (2016)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/neundorf-niemi-smets-2016-compensation-effect-civic-education/) — civic education as a compensatory socialization channel
- [Achen & Bartels (2016)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/achen-bartels-2016-democracy-for-realists/) — argues information does little to change identity-driven vote choice, a skeptical bookend to the correct-voting literature; questions whether informing voters meaningfully changes outcomes
- [Augenblick & Nicholson (2016)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/augenblick-nicholson-2016-choice-fatigue/) — establishes that roll-off is partly a cognitive-cost/fatigue artifact of ballot length itself, independent of how informed a voter is — which bounds what information tools alone can fix
- [Jones et al. (2017)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/jones-2017-social-influence-2012-election/) — reinforces that social mobilization cues, not information, produce the effect
- [Flynn, Nyhan & Reifler (2017)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/flynn-nyhan-reifler-2017-nature-origins-misperceptions/) — theorizes how motivated reasoning keeps beliefs (and thus vote choice) misaligned with the facts
- [Barber et al. (2017)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/barber-2017-status-quo-bias-ballot-wording/) — shows misleading framing in ballot wording pushes votes away from underlying preferences
- [Kalla & Broockman (2018)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/kalla-broockman-2018-minimal-persuasive-effects/) — cornerstone "minimal effects" statement distinguishing persuasion from mobilization
- [Fahey, Weissert & Uttermark (2018)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/fahey-weissert-uttermark-2018-newspaper-endorsements-rolloff/) — distinguishes persuasion effects from participation/completion effects of information
- [Boudreau, Elmendorf & MacKenzie (2019)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/boudreau-elmendorf-mackenzie-2019-roadmaps-to-representation/) — direct evidence that education tools move votes toward voters' own preferences
- [Coppock, Hill & Vavreck (2020)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/coppock-hill-vavreck-2020-small-effects-advertising/) — supports the minimal-persuasion view of campaign messaging
- [Kalla & Broockman (2020)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/kalla-broockman-2020-reducing-exclusionary-attitudes/) — argument-only (informational) contact failed while perspective-taking succeeded
- [Holbein & Hillygus (2020)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/holbein-hillygus-2020-making-young-voters/) — locates the youth problem in follow-through, not information or interest
- [Singh (2021)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/singh-2021-beyond-turnout/) — shows compelled voters vote less on ideology/information, central to the quality-vs-turnout debate
- [Boudreau, Colner & MacKenzie (2021)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/boudreau-colner-mackenzie-2021-voter-guides-narrow-gap/) — shows information tools can move uninformed voters' choices closer to their policy views, improving the match between votes and preferences; an information intervention that changes the quality and expression of votes rather than turnout
- [Coppock, Green & Porter (2022)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/coppock-green-porter-2022-digital-advertising-vote-choice/) — evidence that digital ad exposure does not shift electoral outcomes
- [Aggarwal et al. (2023)](/voter-education-turnout-wiki/research/aggarwal-2023-campaign-wide-digital-advertising-turnout/) — digital advertising did not mobilize turnout at scale
